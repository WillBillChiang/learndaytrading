MODULE 04

Technical Indicators

Technical indicators are mathematical calculations based on price, volume, or open interest. They help traders quantify trends, measure momentum, identify overbought/oversold conditions, and spot potential reversals. Used correctly, they add a powerful analytical layer to your chart reading — but they are tools, not crystal balls.

4.1 Why Use Indicators

Before diving into specific indicators, it is critical to understand their role and limitations. Indicators are derived from price and volume data — they do not predict the future. They process information that is already on the chart to make patterns and trends more visible.

Indicators Are Tools, Not Crystal Balls

No indicator is right 100% of the time. Every indicator will produce false signals. The value lies in combining indicators with price action, volume, and market context to build a higher-probability trading thesis. Treat indicators as one piece of evidence, not as a verdict.

Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

This is one of the most important distinctions in technical analysis. Understanding the difference will help you choose the right tool for the right situation.

Category Behavior Best For Examples Weakness
Leading (Oscillators) Signal before a move happens Anticipating reversals RSI, Stochastic, CCI Frequent false signals in trending markets
Lagging (Trend-Following) Confirm after a move begins Riding established trends Moving Averages, MACD Late entries; miss the early portion of moves
Indicator Overload

A common beginner mistake is loading 8–10 indicators on a chart, creating confusion and conflicting signals. Stick to 2–3 complementary indicators maximum. For example, one trend indicator (moving average) plus one momentum oscillator (RSI) plus volume is a solid combination.

Choosing Your Indicator Mix

The best approach is to combine indicators from different categories so they provide different types of information:

  • Trend identification: Moving averages or MACD tell you the direction
  • Momentum / timing: RSI or Stochastic tell you when a move may be exhausted
  • Volatility: Bollinger Bands tell you how much movement to expect
  • Confirmation: Volume or VWAP validates whether the move is genuine

4.2 Moving Averages

Moving averages are the most widely used technical indicators. They smooth out price data to create a single flowing line that makes trend direction easier to identify. Every professional trader watches at least one or two moving averages.

Simple Moving Average (SMA)

The SMA calculates the arithmetic mean of the last n closing prices.

SMA Formula

SMA = (P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ... + Pₙ) / n

For example, a 20-period SMA adds the last 20 closing prices and divides by 20. Each new bar, the oldest price drops off and the newest one is added.

Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

The EMA gives more weight to recent prices, making it more responsive to new information.

EMA Formula

EMA = Price × k + EMA_prev × (1 - k)
where k = 2 / (n + 1)

Because of the weighting, EMAs react faster to price changes than SMAs of the same period. This makes EMAs better for fast-moving intraday trading, while SMAs are better for identifying longer-term trends.

Key Moving Average Periods

Period Common Use Time Horizon
9 EMA Very short-term momentum, scalping Intraday
20 SMA/EMA Short-term trend, pullback entries Swing / Intraday
50 SMA Intermediate trend, institutional level Swing
200 SMA Long-term trend, bull/bear market divider Position / Macro

Crossover Strategies

When a shorter-period moving average crosses above or below a longer-period one, it generates a signal. These are some of the most widely watched events in all of trading:

Golden Cross

The 50 SMA crosses above the 200 SMA. This is a major bullish signal that indicates the intermediate trend is now stronger than the long-term trend was. Institutions watch this closely.

Example: After a bear market bottom, the 50 SMA catching up and crossing above the 200 SMA often signals the beginning of a new bull phase.

Death Cross

The 50 SMA crosses below the 200 SMA. This is a major bearish signal. It indicates that the intermediate trend has turned negative relative to the long-term trend, suggesting further downside.

Example: A death cross in a major index like the S&P 500 can trigger widespread selling as algorithmic systems react to the signal.

Golden Cross: the 50 SMA crossing above the 200 SMA is a major bullish signal
Day Trading Moving Average Strategy

Many day traders use the 9 EMA and 20 EMA on a 5-minute chart. When the 9 EMA crosses above the 20 EMA, they look for long entries on pullbacks to the 9 EMA. When the 9 EMA crosses below the 20 EMA, they look for short entries. Simple, but effective when combined with volume and price action.

4.3 RSI (Relative Strength Index)

Developed by J. Welles Wilder in 1978, the RSI is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and magnitude of price changes. It oscillates between 0 and 100, giving you a quick read on whether an asset is overbought or oversold.

RSI Formula Concept

RSI = 100 - (100 / (1 + RS))
where RS = Average Gain / Average Loss over n periods (default 14)

Key RSI Levels

Level Reading Interpretation Action
Above 70 Overbought Price has risen aggressively; may be due for a pullback Watch for reversal signals; consider taking profits on longs
50 (midline) Neutral Equilibrium point; acts as support in uptrends, resistance in downtrends Use as trend confirmation tool
Below 30 Oversold Price has dropped aggressively; may be due for a bounce Watch for reversal signals; look for buying opportunities
RSI oscillating between oversold (below 30) and overbought (above 70) zones, shown below the price chart

RSI Divergence

Divergence is when the RSI disagrees with price, and it is one of the most powerful signals RSI produces:

Bullish Divergence

Price makes a lower low, but RSI makes a higher low. This signals that selling momentum is weakening even though price is still falling. Often precedes a reversal upward.

Bearish Divergence

Price makes a higher high, but RSI makes a lower high. This signals that buying momentum is fading even though price keeps rising. Often precedes a reversal downward.

Overbought Does Not Mean “Sell”

A common mistake is treating RSI above 70 as an automatic sell signal. In strong uptrends, RSI can stay overbought for extended periods while price continues to climb. Overbought means stretched, not finished. Wait for a confirming signal like a bearish candle pattern or a break of support before acting.

4.4 MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)

Developed by Gerald Appel, the MACD is a trend-following momentum indicator. It shows the relationship between two exponential moving averages and is one of the most versatile indicators available to traders.

MACD Components

  • MACD Line: 12 EMA - 26 EMA — The difference between the fast and slow EMA. When the MACD line is positive, the short-term trend is above the long-term trend (bullish).
  • Signal Line: 9 EMA of MACD Line — A smoothed version of the MACD line. Crossovers between the MACD line and signal line generate trade signals.
  • Histogram: MACD Line - Signal Line — Visualizes the gap between the two lines. Growing histogram bars indicate increasing momentum; shrinking bars indicate fading momentum.
  • Zero Line: When MACD crosses above zero, the short-term EMA has crossed above the long-term EMA (bullish). Below zero is bearish.
MACD with bullish and bearish crossovers; histogram shows momentum strength

MACD Trading Signals

  • Bullish crossover: MACD line crosses above the signal line — momentum is shifting bullish. Strongest when it occurs below the zero line.
  • Bearish crossover: MACD line crosses below the signal line — momentum is shifting bearish. Strongest when it occurs above the zero line.
  • Zero line crossover: MACD crossing above zero confirms bullish trend; below zero confirms bearish trend.
  • Divergence: Just like RSI, price making new highs while MACD makes lower highs (bearish divergence) or price making new lows while MACD makes higher lows (bullish divergence) are powerful reversal signals.
  • Histogram expansion: Histogram bars growing taller indicate accelerating momentum. Shrinking bars suggest the move is slowing.
MACD for Day Trading

On intraday charts (1-min, 5-min), the standard MACD settings (12, 26, 9) work well. Some traders tighten the settings to (5, 13, 1) for faster signals on shorter time frames, but this increases noise. Start with the defaults and adjust only after gaining experience.

4.5 Bollinger Bands

Created by John Bollinger in the 1980s, Bollinger Bands are a volatility indicator that consists of three lines plotted around a moving average. They dynamically expand and contract based on market volatility, giving you a visual envelope around price.

Bollinger Band Components

  • Middle Band: 20-period SMA — The baseline trend line.
  • Upper Band: Middle Band + (2 × Standard Deviation) — Upper volatility boundary.
  • Lower Band: Middle Band - (2 × Standard Deviation) — Lower volatility boundary.

Statistically, about 95% of price action falls within the bands (2 standard deviations). When price moves outside the bands, it is a statistically significant event.

Bollinger Bands: bands contract during low-volatility squeezes and expand during high-volatility breakouts

Key Bollinger Band Concepts

The Bollinger Squeeze

When the bands narrow significantly (a "squeeze"), it indicates low volatility and typically precedes a strong directional move. The squeeze itself does not tell you which direction price will break, but it tells you that a big move is coming. Watch for the breakout direction and trade with it.

  • Walking the bands: In strong trends, price can "walk" along the upper band (uptrend) or lower band (downtrend) for extended periods. This is not a signal to fade the move.
  • Mean reversion: In ranging markets, price tends to oscillate between the upper and lower bands, reverting to the middle band (SMA). Touches of the outer bands can be entry signals.
  • Band width: The distance between the bands tells you about current volatility. Unusually wide bands suggest volatility may contract soon; unusually narrow bands suggest an imminent breakout.
  • Fake breakouts: Price briefly poking outside a band, then immediately reversing back inside, is often a reversal signal. This is the "Bollinger Bounce."
Bollinger + RSI Combination

A powerful setup: when price touches the lower Bollinger Band and RSI is oversold (below 30), it is a strong potential buy signal. Conversely, price at the upper band with RSI overbought (above 70) is a strong potential sell signal. This combination reduces false signals significantly.

4.6 VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price)

VWAP is the single most important indicator for day traders. It represents the average price a security has traded at throughout the day, weighted by volume. Unlike a simple moving average, VWAP accounts for how much was traded at each price, giving it a gravity-like pull on intraday price action.

VWAP Formula

VWAP = Cumulative(Price × Volume) / Cumulative(Volume)

VWAP resets at the beginning of each trading day. It starts at the opening price and evolves as trades occur throughout the session.

Why Day Traders Use VWAP

  • Institutional benchmark: Large institutions judge their execution quality against VWAP. If they bought below VWAP, they got a "good" fill. This creates real buying/selling pressure around the VWAP line.
  • Fair value reference: VWAP represents the "fair price" at any point during the day. Price trading above VWAP means buyers have been more aggressive; below means sellers dominate.
  • Trend filter: Above VWAP = bullish bias (look for longs). Below VWAP = bearish bias (look for shorts). This simple filter eliminates many bad trades.
  • Dynamic support/resistance: Price will frequently bounce off or reject from the VWAP line, especially in the first few hours of trading.

VWAP Trading Strategies

Strategy Setup Entry Notes
VWAP Bounce Price pulls back to VWAP in an uptrend Buy when price touches VWAP and shows a bullish candle Best in the first 2 hours of the session
VWAP Rejection Price rallies to VWAP from below in a downtrend Short when price reaches VWAP and shows a bearish candle Strongest after a clear opening drive lower
VWAP Reclaim Price trades below VWAP, then breaks back above Buy the reclaim and hold of VWAP Watch for volume surge on the reclaim
VWAP Extension Price extends far from VWAP Fade the extreme when price is 2+ standard deviations from VWAP Mean-reversion play; use tight stops
VWAP is an Intraday-Only Indicator

Because VWAP resets daily, it is meaningless on daily or weekly charts. It is strictly a day trading tool. Do not carry VWAP analysis into swing trade decisions. For multi-day analysis, use anchored VWAP or standard moving averages instead.

The “VWAP or Nothing” Rule

Many professional day traders will not take a trade unless they know where price is relative to VWAP. If you had to pick only one indicator for day trading, VWAP should be it. Add it to your charts before anything else.
