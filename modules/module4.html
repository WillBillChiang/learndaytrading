MODULE 04

Technical Indicators

Technical indicators are mathematical calculations based on price, volume, or open interest. They help traders quantify trends, measure momentum, identify overbought/oversold conditions, and spot potential reversals. Used correctly, they add a powerful analytical layer to your chart reading — but they are tools, not crystal balls.

4.1 Why Use Indicators

Before diving into specific indicators, it is critical to understand their role and limitations. Indicators are derived from price and volume data — they do not predict the future. They process information that is already on the chart to make patterns and trends more visible.

Indicators Are Tools, Not Crystal Balls

No indicator is right 100% of the time. Every indicator will produce false signals. The value lies in combining indicators with price action, volume, and market context to build a higher-probability trading thesis. Treat indicators as one piece of evidence, not as a verdict.

Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

This is one of the most important distinctions in technical analysis. Understanding the difference will help you choose the right tool for the right situation.

Category Behavior Best For Examples Weakness
Leading (Oscillators) Signal before a move happens Anticipating reversals RSI, Stochastic, CCI Frequent false signals in trending markets
Lagging (Trend-Following) Confirm after a move begins Riding established trends Moving Averages, MACD Late entries; miss the early portion of moves
Indicator Overload

A common beginner mistake is loading 8–10 indicators on a chart, creating confusion and conflicting signals. Stick to 2–3 complementary indicators maximum. For example, one trend indicator (moving average) plus one momentum oscillator (RSI) plus volume is a solid combination.

Choosing Your Indicator Mix

The best approach is to combine indicators from different categories so they provide different types of information:

  • Trend identification: Moving averages or MACD tell you the direction
  • Momentum / timing: RSI or Stochastic tell you when a move may be exhausted
  • Volatility: Bollinger Bands tell you how much movement to expect
  • Confirmation: Volume or VWAP validates whether the move is genuine

4.2 Moving Averages

Moving averages are the most widely used technical indicators. They smooth out price data to create a single flowing line that makes trend direction easier to identify. Every professional trader watches at least one or two moving averages.

Simple Moving Average (SMA)

The SMA calculates the arithmetic mean of the last n closing prices.

SMA Formula

SMA = (P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ... + Pₙ) / n

For example, a 20-period SMA adds the last 20 closing prices and divides by 20. Each new bar, the oldest price drops off and the newest one is added.

Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

The EMA gives more weight to recent prices, making it more responsive to new information.

EMA Formula

EMA = Price × k + EMA_prev × (1 - k)
where k = 2 / (n + 1)

Because of the weighting, EMAs react faster to price changes than SMAs of the same period. This makes EMAs better for fast-moving intraday trading, while SMAs are better for identifying longer-term trends.

Key Moving Average Periods

Period Common Use Time Horizon
9 EMA Very short-term momentum, scalping Intraday
20 SMA/EMA Short-term trend, pullback entries Swing / Intraday
50 SMA Intermediate trend, institutional level Swing
200 SMA Long-term trend, bull/bear market divider Position / Macro

Crossover Strategies

When a shorter-period moving average crosses above or below a longer-period one, it generates a signal. These are some of the most widely watched events in all of trading:

Golden Cross

The 50 SMA crosses above the 200 SMA. This is a major bullish signal that indicates the intermediate trend is now stronger than the long-term trend was. Institutions watch this closely.

Example: After a bear market bottom, the 50 SMA catching up and crossing above the 200 SMA often signals the beginning of a new bull phase.

Death Cross

The 50 SMA crosses below the 200 SMA. This is a major bearish signal. It indicates that the intermediate trend has turned negative relative to the long-term trend, suggesting further downside.

Example: A death cross in a major index like the S&P 500 can trigger widespread selling as algorithmic systems react to the signal.

Golden Cross: the 50 SMA crossing above the 200 SMA is a major bullish signal
Day Trading Moving Average Strategy

Many day traders use the 9 EMA and 20 EMA on a 5-minute chart. When the 9 EMA crosses above the 20 EMA, they look for long entries on pullbacks to the 9 EMA. When the 9 EMA crosses below the 20 EMA, they look for short entries. Simple, but effective when combined with volume and price action.

4.3 RSI (Relative Strength Index)

Developed by J. Welles Wilder in 1978, the RSI is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and magnitude of price changes. It oscillates between 0 and 100, giving you a quick read on whether an asset is overbought or oversold.

RSI Formula Concept

RSI = 100 - (100 / (1 + RS))
where RS = Average Gain / Average Loss over n periods (default 14)

Key RSI Levels

Level Reading Interpretation Action
Above 70 Overbought Price has risen aggressively; may be due for a pullback Watch for reversal signals; consider taking profits on longs
50 (midline) Neutral Equilibrium point; acts as support in uptrends, resistance in downtrends Use as trend confirmation tool
Below 30 Oversold Price has dropped aggressively; may be due for a bounce Watch for reversal signals; look for buying opportunities
RSI oscillating between oversold (below 30) and overbought (above 70) zones, shown below the price chart

RSI Divergence

Divergence is when the RSI disagrees with price, and it is one of the most powerful signals RSI produces:

Bullish Divergence

Price makes a lower low, but RSI makes a higher low. This signals that selling momentum is weakening even though price is still falling. Often precedes a reversal upward.

Bearish Divergence

Price makes a higher high, but RSI makes a lower high. This signals that buying momentum is fading even though price keeps rising. Often precedes a reversal downward.

Hidden Divergence vs. Regular Divergence

Most traders learn regular divergence, but hidden divergence is equally important — and often more profitable because it trades with the trend rather than against it. The two types serve completely different purposes.

Type Price Action RSI Action Signal Best Context
Regular Bullish Lower low Higher low Potential reversal UP At support, after extended downtrend
Regular Bearish Higher high Lower high Potential reversal DOWN At resistance, after extended uptrend
Hidden Bullish Higher low Lower low Trend continuation UP During pullback in an uptrend
Hidden Bearish Lower high Higher high Trend continuation DOWN During bounce in a downtrend

Hidden divergence is a trend continuation signal: it tells you the pullback is losing momentum and the main trend is about to resume. Regular divergence is a reversal signal: it tells you the current trend is weakening. Many professional traders prefer hidden divergence because trading with the trend has a higher base rate of success than catching reversals.

Overbought Does Not Mean “Sell”

A common mistake is treating RSI above 70 as an automatic sell signal. In strong uptrends, RSI can stay overbought for extended periods while price continues to climb. Overbought means stretched, not finished. Wait for a confirming signal like a bearish candle pattern or a break of support before acting.

4.4 MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)

Developed by Gerald Appel, the MACD is a trend-following momentum indicator. It shows the relationship between two exponential moving averages and is one of the most versatile indicators available to traders.

MACD Components

  • MACD Line: 12 EMA - 26 EMA — The difference between the fast and slow EMA. When the MACD line is positive, the short-term trend is above the long-term trend (bullish).
  • Signal Line: 9 EMA of MACD Line — A smoothed version of the MACD line. Crossovers between the MACD line and signal line generate trade signals.
  • Histogram: MACD Line - Signal Line — Visualizes the gap between the two lines. Growing histogram bars indicate increasing momentum; shrinking bars indicate fading momentum.
  • Zero Line: When MACD crosses above zero, the short-term EMA has crossed above the long-term EMA (bullish). Below zero is bearish.
MACD with bullish and bearish crossovers; histogram shows momentum strength

MACD Trading Signals

  • Bullish crossover: MACD line crosses above the signal line — momentum is shifting bullish. Strongest when it occurs below the zero line.
  • Bearish crossover: MACD line crosses below the signal line — momentum is shifting bearish. Strongest when it occurs above the zero line.
  • Zero line crossover: MACD crossing above zero confirms bullish trend; below zero confirms bearish trend.
  • Divergence: Just like RSI, price making new highs while MACD makes lower highs (bearish divergence) or price making new lows while MACD makes higher lows (bullish divergence) are powerful reversal signals.
  • Histogram expansion: Histogram bars growing taller indicate accelerating momentum. Shrinking bars suggest the move is slowing.
MACD for Day Trading

On intraday charts (1-min, 5-min), the standard MACD settings (12, 26, 9) work well. Some traders tighten the settings to (5, 13, 1) for faster signals on shorter time frames, but this increases noise. Start with the defaults and adjust only after gaining experience.

4.5 Bollinger Bands

Created by John Bollinger in the 1980s, Bollinger Bands are a volatility indicator that consists of three lines plotted around a moving average. They dynamically expand and contract based on market volatility, giving you a visual envelope around price.

Bollinger Band Components

  • Middle Band: 20-period SMA — The baseline trend line.
  • Upper Band: Middle Band + (2 × Standard Deviation) — Upper volatility boundary.
  • Lower Band: Middle Band - (2 × Standard Deviation) — Lower volatility boundary.

Statistically, about 95% of price action falls within the bands (2 standard deviations). When price moves outside the bands, it is a statistically significant event.

Bollinger Bands: bands contract during low-volatility squeezes and expand during high-volatility breakouts

Key Bollinger Band Concepts

The Bollinger Squeeze

When the bands narrow significantly (a "squeeze"), it indicates low volatility and typically precedes a strong directional move. The squeeze itself does not tell you which direction price will break, but it tells you that a big move is coming. Watch for the breakout direction and trade with it.

  • Walking the bands: In strong trends, price can "walk" along the upper band (uptrend) or lower band (downtrend) for extended periods. This is not a signal to fade the move.
  • Mean reversion: In ranging markets, price tends to oscillate between the upper and lower bands, reverting to the middle band (SMA). Touches of the outer bands can be entry signals.
  • Band width: The distance between the bands tells you about current volatility. Unusually wide bands suggest volatility may contract soon; unusually narrow bands suggest an imminent breakout.
  • Fake breakouts: Price briefly poking outside a band, then immediately reversing back inside, is often a reversal signal. This is the "Bollinger Bounce."

Bollinger Band Width (BBW) — Quantifying the Squeeze

While you can visually identify a Bollinger squeeze, the Bollinger Band Width indicator provides a precise numerical measurement. It removes subjectivity from squeeze detection.

Formula: BBW = (Upper Band - Lower Band) / Middle Band

BBW gives you a single number representing the width of the bands relative to the moving average. By tracking BBW over time, you can identify historically extreme compression — the setups most likely to produce explosive breakouts.

BBW Percentile Interpretation Trading Implication
Below 10th percentile Extreme compression — volatility at historic lows for this stock High-probability breakout imminent. Set alerts and prepare entries in both directions.
10th–30th percentile Low volatility — a squeeze is forming Start watching for the squeeze breakout. Tighten your scanner criteria for this stock.
30th–70th percentile Normal volatility — no special signal Standard trading conditions. Use other indicators for trade signals.
Above 70th percentile High volatility — bands are wide Volatility may contract soon. Consider mean-reversion setups. Widen stops to account for noise.
Above 90th percentile Extreme expansion — often seen after earnings, news events Avoid breakout strategies. Look for exhaustion signals and mean reversion back to the middle band.
Bollinger + RSI Combination

A powerful setup: when price touches the lower Bollinger Band and RSI is oversold (below 30), it is a strong potential buy signal. Conversely, price at the upper band with RSI overbought (above 70) is a strong potential sell signal. This combination reduces false signals significantly.

4.6 VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price)

VWAP is the single most important indicator for day traders. It represents the average price a security has traded at throughout the day, weighted by volume. Unlike a simple moving average, VWAP accounts for how much was traded at each price, giving it a gravity-like pull on intraday price action.

VWAP Formula

VWAP = Cumulative(Price × Volume) / Cumulative(Volume)

VWAP resets at the beginning of each trading day. It starts at the opening price and evolves as trades occur throughout the session.

Why Day Traders Use VWAP

  • Institutional benchmark: Large institutions judge their execution quality against VWAP. If they bought below VWAP, they got a "good" fill. This creates real buying/selling pressure around the VWAP line.
  • Fair value reference: VWAP represents the "fair price" at any point during the day. Price trading above VWAP means buyers have been more aggressive; below means sellers dominate.
  • Trend filter: Above VWAP = bullish bias (look for longs). Below VWAP = bearish bias (look for shorts). This simple filter eliminates many bad trades.
  • Dynamic support/resistance: Price will frequently bounce off or reject from the VWAP line, especially in the first few hours of trading.

VWAP Trading Strategies

Strategy Setup Entry Notes
VWAP Bounce Price pulls back to VWAP in an uptrend Buy when price touches VWAP and shows a bullish candle Best in the first 2 hours of the session
VWAP Rejection Price rallies to VWAP from below in a downtrend Short when price reaches VWAP and shows a bearish candle Strongest after a clear opening drive lower
VWAP Reclaim Price trades below VWAP, then breaks back above Buy the reclaim and hold of VWAP Watch for volume surge on the reclaim
VWAP Extension Price extends far from VWAP Fade the extreme when price is 2+ standard deviations from VWAP Mean-reversion play; use tight stops
VWAP is an Intraday-Only Indicator

Because VWAP resets daily, it is meaningless on daily or weekly charts. It is strictly a day trading tool. Do not carry VWAP analysis into swing trade decisions. For multi-day analysis, use anchored VWAP or standard moving averages instead.

The “VWAP or Nothing” Rule

Many professional day traders will not take a trade unless they know where price is relative to VWAP. If you had to pick only one indicator for day trading, VWAP should be it. Add it to your charts before anything else.

4.8 Market Internals Indicators

Market internals are breadth and volume-based indicators that measure the overall health of the market in real time. Unlike indicators applied to a single stock, internals tell you what the entire market is doing under the surface. For day traders focused on broad ETFs like SPY and QQQ, market internals are an indispensable dashboard that reveals whether the market environment favors longs, shorts, or sitting on your hands.

NYSE TICK Index

The NYSE TICK index is a real-time measure of market sentiment. It calculates the net number of NYSE stocks ticking up minus those ticking down at any given moment. Every time a stock trades at a price higher than its previous trade, that is an "uptick." Every trade at a lower price is a "downtick." The TICK index sums these across all NYSE-listed stocks.

  • Range: The TICK typically oscillates between -2000 and +2000, with most readings between -800 and +800. Increased algorithmic participation and market structure changes since the late 2010s have widened the historical range significantly (earlier references often cite ±1200, which is outdated).
  • Extreme readings: Readings above +1000 indicate strong, broad-based buying pressure. Readings below -1000 indicate aggressive, widespread selling. These extremes often correspond to short-term inflection points. On highly volatile days (FOMC, CPI), readings can exceed ±2000.
  • Cumulative TICK: Tracking the running sum of TICK readings throughout the day gives you a broader picture of whether buyers or sellers are dominating the session. A rising cumulative TICK favors longs; a falling cumulative TICK favors shorts.
  • TICK divergence: If SPY is making new intraday highs but the TICK readings are getting weaker (lower highs), the rally lacks breadth and may be about to reverse.
TICK Reading Interpretation Day Trading Action
Above +1200 Extreme buying — institutional programs firing aggressively Look for long entries on pullbacks; avoid initiating new shorts
+600 to +1200 Moderate bullish breadth — buyers in control Favor long setups; use TICK pullbacks to -200 as entries
-600 to +600 Neutral — choppy, no clear directional conviction Reduce position size; wait for extremes or trade ranges
-1200 to -600 Moderate bearish breadth — sellers in control Favor short setups; use TICK bounces to +200 as entries
Below -1200 Extreme selling — panic or institutional liquidation Look for short entries on bounces; avoid initiating new longs. Also watch for snapback reversal if selling is exhausted.

ADD — Advance-Decline Line

The Advance-Decline line (ADD or $ADD on most platforms) tracks the cumulative difference between the number of advancing stocks (stocks up on the day) and declining stocks (stocks down on the day) on the NYSE. It is the premier breadth indicator.

  • Confirming moves: When SPY rallies and the ADD is trending higher, the rally has broad participation and is more likely to sustain. When SPY rallies but the ADD is flat or falling, the rally is narrow and fragile.
  • Divergence signals: ADD making lower highs while SPY makes higher highs is a bearish divergence—the advance is being led by fewer and fewer stocks.
  • Zero line significance: When the ADD crosses from negative to positive territory during the day, it signals a shift in control from sellers to buyers and often coincides with a tradeable reversal in SPY.

VOLD — Up Volume vs. Down Volume

VOLD measures the total volume of advancing stocks minus the total volume of declining stocks on the NYSE. While ADD counts the number of stocks going up or down, VOLD measures the dollar-weighted conviction behind those moves.

  • Reading VOLD: A positive and rising VOLD means more volume is flowing into advancing stocks—the buying is real and backed by size. Negative and falling VOLD means heavy selling volume dominates.
  • Confirmation with ADD: When both ADD and VOLD are positive and trending together, the market has strong bullish internals. When they diverge (e.g., ADD positive but VOLD negative), the advance is suspicious—many stocks are up, but the volume is not behind them.
  • Extreme VOLD readings: VOLD values exceeding +3 billion or below -3 billion indicate very strong conviction days. On these days, trading with the trend and avoiding counter-trend trades is the highest-probability approach.
Building Your Internals Dashboard

Set up a secondary monitor or a panel in your charting platform with three charts stacked vertically: TICK (as a line chart), ADD (as a line chart), and VOLD (as a line chart). Before every trade, glance at this dashboard. If all three are aligned in the same direction as your trade idea, you have a green light. If they diverge from your setup, reduce size or skip the trade entirely.

Internals Are for Index/ETF Trading

Market internals are most useful when trading broad market instruments like SPY, QQQ, ES, and NQ futures. Individual stocks can move against the market, so internals are a supporting factor—not the primary driver—when trading single names. For SPY and QQQ day trading, however, internals are essential.

4.9 Volume Profile

Most traders are familiar with volume bars at the bottom of a chart, which show how much was traded during each time period. Volume Profile flips that concept on its side: it displays volume as a horizontal histogram along the price axis, showing you at which prices the most trading occurred. This reveals where the market found agreement (high volume) and where it moved quickly through (low volume).

Key Volume Profile Levels

  • Point of Control (POC): The single price level where the most volume was traded during the selected period. The POC acts as a magnet—price is attracted to it and tends to spend time there. It represents the "fairest" price where buyers and sellers found the most agreement.
  • Value Area (VA): The price range containing approximately 70% of all volume traded. This is based on the first standard deviation of the volume distribution. The Value Area represents where the majority of business was conducted.
  • Value Area High (VAH): The upper boundary of the Value Area. Acts as resistance when price approaches from below and support when price is trading above it.
  • Value Area Low (VAL): The lower boundary of the Value Area. Acts as support when price approaches from above and resistance when price is trading below it.
  • High Volume Nodes (HVN): Price levels with significantly more volume than surrounding levels. These act as support/resistance zones because many participants have positions there and will defend them.
  • Low Volume Nodes (LVN): Price levels with very little volume—"air pockets" where price moved quickly. Price tends to move rapidly through LVNs and slow down at HVNs.
Volume Profile histogram showing POC, Value Area (VAH/VAL), High Volume Nodes, and Low Volume Nodes alongside price action

Naked POC — Prior Session Levels

A Naked POC is the Point of Control from a prior trading session that has not yet been revisited by price. Because the POC represents the price of maximum agreement, it acts as a powerful magnet. The market has a strong tendency to "fill" or revisit naked POCs, sometimes days or even weeks later.

  • Trading naked POCs: Mark the POC from each prior session on your chart. When price approaches a naked POC, expect it to act as support or resistance. Many traders use naked POCs as profit targets or areas to tighten stops.
  • Multiple naked POCs: If several sessions' POCs cluster near the same price, that level becomes extremely significant—a high-probability zone for a reaction.
  • Once tested: After price touches and trades through a naked POC, it is "dressed" and loses much of its magnetic pull. Remove it or reduce its importance on your chart.
Volume Profile for Support & Resistance

Traditional support and resistance use horizontal lines at price swing points. Volume Profile adds a deeper layer: it shows you where the market actually transacted the most business. A high-volume node is a stronger support/resistance level than a simple price touch because it represents a zone where thousands of participants built positions and have a vested interest in defending that price.

Volume Profile Time Frames

You can apply Volume Profile to different time ranges: a single session (session profile), a fixed range of bars (fixed range), or a visible range on your chart (visible range volume profile, or VRVP). For day trading, use the prior session's profile to identify key levels, and the developing session's profile to see where volume is building in real time.

Deriving Price Targets from Volume Profile

Volume Profile is not just for support and resistance — it provides precise price targets based on where the market has actually transacted. Here are three Volume Profile targeting techniques used by professional traders:

  • Low Volume Node (LVN) as Magnet: When price breaks out of a High Volume Node, it tends to accelerate through Low Volume Nodes (areas where little trading occurred) and decelerate at the next HVN. Use the next HVN as your price target. Price moves through LVNs like a ball rolling through a valley — quickly and with little resistance.
  • Prior-Day POC as Target: Yesterday’s Point of Control (POC) is a natural magnet for today’s price. If today’s opening is above yesterday’s POC, use it as a downside target for short trades. If below, use it as an upside target for long trades. Studies show price tests the prior-day POC on approximately 80% of trading days.
  • Value Area Rotation: If price opens inside the prior session’s Value Area (between VAH and VAL), expect a rotation between VAH and VAL. If price opens outside the Value Area and re-enters it, target the opposite end of the Value Area. This is called a Value Area rotation trade — one of the most reliable Volume Profile setups.
  • Naked (Untested) POC: A POC from a prior session that has never been retested is called a “naked POC.” These act as strong magnets for future price. Naked POCs from 2, 5, or even 20+ sessions ago remain valid until tested. They make excellent targets for swing-style day trades.

4.10 Anchored VWAP

In Section 4.6 you learned about standard VWAP, which resets every day. Anchored VWAP (AVWAP) takes this concept further by allowing you to anchor the VWAP calculation to any specific bar or event you choose. This gives you a dynamic support/resistance line that reflects the volume-weighted average price from a meaningful starting point—an earnings report, a gap day, a swing high, a swing low, or any event that changed the character of the stock.

How Anchored VWAP Differs from Regular VWAP

Regular VWAP resets at the start of each session and is useful only for intraday analysis. Anchored VWAP does not reset—it continuously accumulates the volume-weighted average from whatever starting point you select. This means AVWAP can span days, weeks, or even months, making it useful for swing traders and position traders as well as day traders.

  • Regular VWAP: Answers "What is the average price paid today?"
  • Anchored VWAP: Answers "What is the average price paid since [specific event]?"

Because AVWAP starts from a specific event, it captures the positions of all participants who entered the market around that event. This makes it a window into where those participants are likely feeling pain or profit.

Where to Anchor VWAP

  • Earnings announcements: Anchor VWAP to the first bar after an earnings report. This level reveals the average price of everyone who traded on the new fundamental information. If price pulls back to this AVWAP and holds, it confirms the market still values the stock at post-earnings prices.
  • Gap days: Anchor to the open of a significant gap up or gap down. Traders who bought the gap are underwater if price drops below this AVWAP—they will sell to break even, creating resistance.
  • Swing highs and lows: Anchor to major turning points. The AVWAP from a swing high represents the average entry of everyone who bought during the decline from that peak—a natural resistance level.
  • IPO or first trade date: The AVWAP from a stock's first trading day shows the average price of all participants since inception.
  • Fed/FOMC announcements: Anchor to the bar when major macroeconomic data was released. This reveals how the market has repriced relative to that information.

Brian Shannon & Institutional Use of AVWAP

Brian Shannon, author of Technical Analysis Using Multiple Timeframes and a pioneer of AVWAP analysis, popularized the use of Anchored VWAP as a primary trading tool. His core insight is that AVWAP reveals the "line in the sand" for institutional participants.

  • Institutional average cost basis: Large funds that accumulate positions over days or weeks have an average entry price that closely tracks the AVWAP from when they started buying. If price drops to their AVWAP, they often add to their position (support). If price rises well above it, they may trim (resistance).
  • Multi-timeframe AVWAP: Shannon recommends plotting multiple AVWAPs simultaneously—from the most recent swing low, the prior earnings date, and the yearly low. When price sits above all three, the trend is firmly bullish. When AVWAPs converge at one price, that level is extremely significant.
  • AVWAP as a stop-loss reference: Traders can use an AVWAP as a trailing stop. As long as price holds above the AVWAP from the most recent significant low, the trend is intact. A close below it signals a potential trend change.
AVWAP Confluence = High Probability

When an Anchored VWAP from one event converges with an AVWAP from a different event at the same price, you have a powerful confluence zone. For example, if the AVWAP from earnings and the AVWAP from the most recent swing low both sit at $150, that level is extremely likely to produce a reaction. Add a Volume Profile POC at the same price and you have a near-perfect support/resistance level.

AVWAP on Most Platforms

Most modern charting platforms (TradingView, Thinkorswim, DAS Trader) support Anchored VWAP. In TradingView, select the Anchored VWAP tool from the drawing toolbar, then click on the candle you want to anchor to. In Thinkorswim, you can add a custom study or use the built-in Anchored VWAP study. Start by anchoring to the most recent earnings date and the most recent significant swing low.

4.11 Fibonacci Extensions & Clusters

You may already be familiar with Fibonacci retracements—levels where pullbacks tend to find support (38.2%, 50%, 61.8%). But Fibonacci extensions go beyond the original move to project where price might travel after a retracement completes. Extensions are essential for setting profit targets in trending markets and identifying where breakout moves are likely to stall or reverse.

Fibonacci Extension Levels

Fibonacci extensions are drawn using three points: a swing low, a swing high, and the retracement low. They project the following key levels beyond the original swing high:

  • 1.272 (127.2%): The first extension level. A conservative profit target for the continuation after a retracement. Commonly hit in moderate trends.
  • 1.618 (161.8%): The "golden ratio" extension. The most widely watched and respected extension level. Price frequently stalls or reverses here. This is the primary profit target for most Fibonacci traders.
  • 2.0 (200%): The measured move or "equal legs" target. If the original swing was $10, the 2.0 extension projects price will travel another $10 from the retracement low. Common in impulsive, momentum-driven markets.
  • 2.618 (261.8%): An extreme extension level reached only in very strong trends. When price reaches 2.618, the move is typically over-extended and due for a significant correction. This is the "stretch target" for parabolic moves.
Extension Level Typical Use Strength as Target
1.272 First profit target; partial position exit Moderate — often tested but may not hold if trend is strong
1.618 Primary profit target; most common reversal point Strong — the golden ratio; highest-probability reaction zone
2.0 Measured move target; used in impulsive trends Strong — represents symmetry; institutions watch this level
2.618 Extended target; parabolic moves only Very Strong as reversal — rarely exceeded; often marks blow-off tops/bottoms

Fibonacci Clusters — Confluence Zones

A Fibonacci cluster occurs when multiple Fibonacci levels from different swing measurements converge at the same price zone. This is one of the most powerful concepts in technical analysis because it combines independent measurements that all point to the same level.

  • How clusters form: Draw Fibonacci retracements and extensions from multiple swings on your chart—the primary trend, a secondary swing, and an intermediate pullback. Where levels from these different measurements land within a tight price range (e.g., within 0.5%), you have a Fibonacci cluster.
  • Example: Suppose the 61.8% retracement of a major swing down sits at $148.50, the 1.272 extension of a smaller bullish swing targets $148.80, and the 50% retracement of an intermediate move falls at $148.60. All three levels are within 30 cents of each other. This $148.50–$148.80 zone is a Fibonacci cluster and a very high-probability area for a price reaction.
  • Why clusters work: Each Fibonacci level represents a group of traders watching that particular swing. When multiple groups are watching the same price zone from different perspectives, order flow converges there—creating a self-reinforcing support or resistance zone.

Combining Fibonacci with Other Tools

  • Fibonacci + Volume Profile: When a Fibonacci extension level aligns with a high-volume node or naked POC from Volume Profile, the level is exceptionally strong. The Fibonacci math and the actual volume data both agree—this is institutional-grade confluence.
  • Fibonacci + AVWAP: If the 1.618 extension coincides with an Anchored VWAP from earnings, the level has both mathematical and volume-weighted significance. These are the setups that produce the cleanest reactions.
  • Fibonacci + round numbers: Psychological price levels ($100, $150, $200, $500) that align with Fibonacci levels gain extra significance because retail and institutional orders cluster at round numbers.
  • Fibonacci + moving averages: A Fibonacci level that coincides with the 50 SMA or 200 SMA on the daily chart is a "triple threat" zone that many different types of traders are watching simultaneously.
The Power of Confluence

No single Fibonacci level is magic. Its power comes from confluence—the more independent technical factors that align at the same price, the higher the probability of a reaction. When you find a Fibonacci cluster that also aligns with a Volume Profile POC and an Anchored VWAP, you are looking at the highest-probability level on your chart. These are the levels to set alerts on and plan your trades around.

Avoid Fibonacci Overload

If you draw Fibonacci levels from every possible swing, your chart will be covered in lines and every price will appear to be "at a level." Be selective: use only the most obvious, highest-volume swings for your measurements. If a level requires squinting to identify the swing points, it is probably not significant enough to trade.
