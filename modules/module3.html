MODULE 03

Chart Patterns & Structure

Every price chart tells a story. Learning to read market structure through support, resistance, trendlines, and classic chart patterns is the foundation of technical analysis. These patterns repeat because they reflect the collective psychology of buyers and sellers — fear, greed, indecision, and conviction.

3.1 Support & Resistance

Support and resistance are the most fundamental concepts in chart analysis. They represent price levels where buying or selling pressure has historically been strong enough to halt or reverse price movement.

What Is Support?

Support is a price level where demand is strong enough to prevent the price from declining further. Think of it as a "floor" that price bounces off. When price approaches support, buyers step in because they perceive the asset as cheap or undervalued at that level.

What Is Resistance?

Resistance is a price level where selling pressure is strong enough to prevent the price from rising further. It acts as a "ceiling." Sellers emerge at resistance because they perceive the asset as expensive or they are taking profits from earlier entries.

Price repeatedly bounces off support (green) and rejects from resistance (red)

What Makes a Strong S/R Level?

Key Factors for S/R Strength

  • Number of touches: The more times price bounces off a level, the stronger it becomes. Three or more touches confirm significance.
  • Recency: Recent levels matter more than levels from months or years ago. Markets have short memories.
  • Volume at the level: High volume at a support or resistance level indicates strong conviction from participants.
  • Time frame: S/R on higher time frames (daily, weekly) is more significant than on lower time frames (1-min, 5-min).
  • Round numbers: Psychological price levels like $100, $50, or $200 often act as natural support/resistance because traders place orders there.
  • Role reversal: When support breaks, it often becomes resistance (and vice versa). This is one of the most powerful concepts in trading.
Pro Tip: Think in Zones, Not Lines

Support and resistance are rarely exact price points. They are zones or areas. A support zone at $50 might extend from $49.50 to $50.50. Drawing them as thin zones rather than precise lines will make your analysis more realistic and reduce false breakout frustration.

How to Identify S/R Levels

  • Look for areas where price has reversed direction multiple times
  • Identify swing highs and swing lows — these naturally form resistance and support
  • Note where large volume clusters appear on a volume profile
  • Watch for gap zones — unfilled gaps often act as support or resistance
  • Use previous day’s high, low, and close as intraday S/R levels

3.2 Trendlines & Channels

While horizontal S/R captures where price stalls, trendlines capture the direction and pace of the current move. A trendline connects a series of higher lows (in an uptrend) or lower highs (in a downtrend), creating a diagonal line that guides price action.

Uptrend Trendline

An uptrend trendline is drawn by connecting two or more higher lows. The line slopes upward from left to right and acts as dynamic support. As long as price remains above the trendline, the uptrend is intact.

Downtrend Trendline

A downtrend trendline connects two or more lower highs. The line slopes downward and acts as dynamic resistance. Price breaking above a downtrend trendline signals a potential trend reversal.

Ascending channel (left) acts as dynamic support; Descending channel (right) acts as dynamic resistance

Trendline Rules

Drawing Valid Trendlines

  • Minimum 3 touches: Two points define a line; the third touch confirms it. More touches = stronger trendline.
  • Steeper = weaker: Very steep trendlines (above 60°) are unsustainable and break quickly. The most reliable trendlines have a moderate slope around 30–45°.
  • Respect the wicks: Generally, connect the wicks (highs/lows) rather than candle bodies for more accurate trendlines.
  • Adjust, don’t force: If price slightly violates a trendline but recovers, it may be worth redrawing rather than declaring it broken.
  • Volume on break: A trendline break accompanied by above-average volume is far more significant than a low-volume violation.
Common Mistake: Over-drawing Trendlines

New traders often force trendlines onto charts to see what they want to see. If you have to "squint" to see the trendline, it probably does not exist. The best trendlines are obvious — anyone looking at the chart should see them.

3.3 Classic Chart Patterns — Reversal

Reversal patterns signal that the current trend is losing momentum and is likely to reverse direction. These patterns develop over time and are most reliable when they appear after a sustained trend. Let us examine the major reversal patterns every day trader should know.

Head & Shoulders Bearish

  • Structure: Three peaks — the middle (head) is the highest, flanked by two lower, roughly equal peaks (shoulders).
  • Neckline: Connect the lows between the left shoulder/head and head/right shoulder. This is your trigger line.
  • Entry: Short when price breaks below the neckline on increased volume.
  • Stop loss: Above the right shoulder.
  • Price target: Measure the distance from the head to the neckline; project that same distance downward from the breakout point.
  • Volume: Volume typically decreases from left shoulder to head to right shoulder, then surges on the neckline break.

Inverse Head & Shoulders Bullish

  • Structure: The mirror image — three troughs, with the middle (head) being the lowest.
  • Entry: Go long when price breaks above the neckline.
  • Stop loss: Below the right shoulder.
  • Price target: Distance from head to neckline projected upward.
  • Volume: Often increases dramatically on the breakout above the neckline.

Double Top Bearish

  • Structure: Two peaks at roughly the same price level, forming an "M" shape.
  • Signal: Bearish — indicates buyers could not push above resistance twice, and momentum is fading.
  • Entry: Short on break below the neckline (the valley between the two peaks).
  • Stop loss: Above the peaks.
  • Price target: Height of the pattern (peak to valley) projected downward from the break.

Double Bottom Bullish

  • Structure: Two troughs at roughly the same price level, forming a "W" shape.
  • Signal: Bullish — sellers could not push price below support twice, indicating a shift to buying.
  • Entry: Long on break above the neckline (the peak between the troughs).
  • Stop loss: Below the troughs.
  • Price target: Height of the pattern projected upward from the breakout point.

Triple Top / Triple Bottom Reversal

  • Structure: Three peaks (triple top) or three troughs (triple bottom) at roughly the same level.
  • Rarer but stronger: Triple tops/bottoms are less common than doubles but signal even stronger reversal conviction.
  • Entry: Same as double top/bottom — on break of the neckline after the third touch.
  • Volume: Typically diminishes on each successive peak/trough and surges on the breakout.
Context Matters

Reversal patterns are only valid when they appear after a sustained trend. A head-and-shoulders that forms in a sideways range carries far less significance than one that caps a multi-week uptrend. Always consider the bigger picture.

3.4 Classic Chart Patterns — Continuation

Continuation patterns form during a pause in the current trend, before price resumes in the same direction. Think of them as the market "catching its breath" before the next leg. These patterns are your opportunity to enter a trending move.

Bull Flag Bullish

  • Structure: A sharp upward move (flagpole) followed by a gentle downward-sloping consolidation channel (flag).
  • Entry: Buy when price breaks above the upper boundary of the flag.
  • Stop loss: Below the lowest point of the flag.
  • Price target: Length of the flagpole projected upward from the breakout.
  • Volume: High volume on the flagpole, declining during the flag, then surging on breakout.

Bear Flag Bearish

  • Structure: A sharp drop (flagpole) followed by a gentle upward-drifting consolidation (flag).
  • Entry: Short when price breaks below the lower boundary of the flag.
  • Price target: Length of the flagpole projected downward.

Pennant Continuation

  • Structure: Like a flag, but the consolidation forms a small symmetrical triangle (converging trendlines) instead of a channel.
  • Duration: Typically short — a few days to two weeks. Longer formations become symmetrical triangles.
  • Entry & target: Same as a flag pattern — break of the pennant boundary with target based on the flagpole length.

Triangles Multiple Types

Triangle Type Flat Side Sloping Side Bias
Ascending Horizontal resistance (top) Rising support (bottom) Bullish
Descending Horizontal support (bottom) Declining resistance (top) Bearish
Symmetric Neither Both converge equally Neutral

Wedges Reversal / Continuation

  • Rising wedge: Both trendlines slope upward, but the lower line is steeper. Looks bullish but is actually bearish — it signals fading upward momentum. Price usually breaks down.
  • Falling wedge: Both trendlines slope downward, but the upper line is steeper. Looks bearish but is actually bullish — selling pressure is exhausting. Price usually breaks up.
  • Key difference from triangles: In wedges, both lines slope in the same direction. In triangles, at least one line is horizontal or they slope in opposite directions.

3.5 Failed Patterns & Trading Broken Setups

One of the most counterintuitive truths in technical analysis: a failed pattern is often a more powerful signal than a successful one. When a well-formed chart pattern breaks in the expected direction and then reverses, it traps traders on the wrong side. These trapped traders are forced to cover, creating explosive moves in the opposite direction. Learning to recognize and trade failed patterns gives you an edge that most retail traders never develop.

Why Failed Patterns Create Explosive Moves

When a textbook head-and-shoulders pattern forms and price breaks the neckline, thousands of traders short the stock. If the breakdown reverses and price reclaims the neckline, every short seller now has a losing position. As they cover (buy back), their buying adds fuel to the reversal. This forced liquidation creates outsized moves — the more traders who were trapped, the more violent the reversal.

  • Trapped traders must exit, adding fuel to the reversal direction
  • Sidelined traders pile in once they see the failure, adding momentum
  • Algorithms detect the stop-loss cascade and trade with the new direction

Rules for Trading Failed Patterns

  • Wait for confirmation: Do not jump in the moment the pattern appears to fail. Wait for a decisive close beyond the pattern’s trigger level in the opposite direction, ideally with above-average volume.
  • Use the pattern as your risk framework: Place your stop loss on the other side of the pattern’s trigger level. If the pattern truly failed, price should not return to the original breakdown/breakout zone.
  • Target the measured move: Use the height of the original pattern as your profit target, projected from the failure point in the reversal direction.
  • Time filter: The best failed patterns reverse within 3–5 bars of the initial break. If price lingers near the trigger level for too long, the failure signal weakens.

Multi-Timeframe Confluence

Failed patterns are most powerful when they occur at levels that matter on higher timeframes. A failed head-and-shoulders on a 5-minute chart is a minor event; a failed head-and-shoulders at a daily support level is a major event. Here is how to use multi-timeframe confluence:

  • Daily chart sets the bias: Identify key support/resistance, trendlines, and moving averages on the daily chart. These are the levels where failed patterns carry the most weight.
  • Hourly chart provides structure: Look for patterns forming near the daily levels. This is where you identify the setup.
  • 5-minute chart provides the entry: Zoom into the 5-minute chart for precise entry timing once the daily/hourly setup is in place. A pattern failure on the 5-minute chart at a daily support level is a high-probability long entry.
  • Volume must confirm across timeframes: High volume on the daily level touch + above-average volume on the 5-minute pattern failure = strong confluence signal.
The Peter Brandt Principle

Legendary chart trader Peter Brandt has noted that failed patterns are among his most profitable setups. His rule: “A pattern that fails to work in the expected direction will often work in the opposite direction with twice the energy.” Track failed patterns in your journal and you will likely find they produce some of your best risk/reward trades.

3.6 Volume Confirmation

Price tells you what happened; volume tells you how strongly the market feels about it. No chart pattern analysis is complete without examining volume. Volume acts as the lie detector of technical analysis — it validates or invalidates every pattern you see.

Volume Rules for Chart Patterns

Scenario Volume Behavior Interpretation
Breakout from resistance High volume surge (1.5x+ average) Strong — Likely genuine breakout
Breakout on low volume Below average Weak — High probability of a fakeout / false breakout
During consolidation pattern Declining / contracting Normal — Expected; coiling = potential energy building

The Breakout Volume Rule

When a chart pattern breaks out, volume should ideally be at least 1.5 to 2 times the recent average. This single confirmation filter can save you from dozens of false breakout trades per month. If volume is not there, sit on your hands and wait.

Volume Divergence

One of the most powerful warning signals is volume divergence — when price makes new highs but volume is declining. This suggests fewer participants are supporting the move, and a reversal may be imminent. Conversely, price making new lows on declining volume can signal a bottom is forming.

Don’t Trade Patterns in Isolation

A perfect-looking head and shoulders with declining volume on the breakdown is likely a false signal. Always combine chart patterns with volume analysis and, ideally, indicator confirmation (covered in Module 4) to increase your probability of success.

3.7 Harmonic Patterns

While classic chart patterns rely on visual recognition and some degree of subjectivity, harmonic patterns are defined by precise Fibonacci ratios. Pioneered by H.M. Gartley in 1935 and later refined by Scott Carney in his Harmonic Trading series, these patterns use specific mathematical relationships between price swings to identify high-probability reversal zones. This mathematical foundation is what sets harmonic patterns apart — they are not open to interpretation the way a head-and-shoulders or wedge might be.

Every harmonic pattern consists of five points labeled X, A, B, C, and D. The legs between these points (XA, AB, BC, CD) must satisfy specific Fibonacci retracement or extension ratios. When the ratios align, the D point forms a Potential Reversal Zone (PRZ) where you expect price to change direction.

The Gartley Pattern (222)

The Gartley is the original harmonic pattern, named after page 222 of H.M. Gartley's 1935 book Profits in the Stock Market. It is the most common harmonic pattern and has a high success rate when the ratios are respected.

  • AB retraces 61.8% of XA
  • BC retraces 38.2% – 88.6% of AB
  • CD extends 127.2% – 161.8% of BC
  • AD retraces 78.6% of XA (the defining ratio)

The 78.6% retracement of XA at point D is the signature of the Gartley. This is where you look for a reversal entry, with a stop loss just beyond X.

The Butterfly Pattern

Discovered by Bryce Gilmore and refined by Scott Carney, the Butterfly is an extension pattern where D extends beyond X. This makes it useful for catching reversals at new extremes.

  • AB retraces 78.6% of XA
  • BC retraces 38.2% – 88.6% of AB
  • CD extends 161.8% – 224% of BC
  • AD extends 127.2% of XA (D goes beyond X)

Because D extends past X, the Butterfly often forms at major market extremes and can capture powerful reversals.

The Bat Pattern

Discovered by Scott Carney in 2001, the Bat pattern is defined by its precise 88.6% retracement at D. Many traders consider this the most reliable harmonic pattern.

  • AB retraces 38.2% – 50% of XA
  • BC retraces 38.2% – 88.6% of AB
  • CD extends 161.8% – 261.8% of BC
  • AD retraces 88.6% of XA (the defining ratio)

The deep 88.6% retracement means price comes very close to X before reversing, giving an excellent risk-to-reward ratio with a tight stop just beyond X.

The Crab Pattern

Also discovered by Scott Carney in 2000, the Crab is the most extended harmonic pattern. Point D reaches 161.8% of XA, making it ideal for catching extreme price spikes and panics.

  • AB retraces 38.2% – 61.8% of XA
  • BC retraces 38.2% – 88.6% of AB
  • CD extends 224% – 361.8% of BC
  • AD extends 161.8% of XA (the defining ratio)

The extreme 161.8% extension at D means the Crab pattern identifies reversals at the most overstretched price levels, often coinciding with climactic volume.

Why Harmonic Patterns Work

Harmonic patterns work because they identify confluences of Fibonacci levels. At point D, multiple Fibonacci ratios from different legs converge into a single zone, creating a concentrated area where institutional algorithms and informed traders place orders. Scott Carney’s research shows that when all ratios align within the specified tolerances, the completion rate at the PRZ exceeds 70%. The key is strict adherence to the ratios — do not force a pattern that does not meet the mathematical criteria.

Precision Is Non-Negotiable

The edge in harmonic trading comes entirely from precision. A pattern where AB retraces 65% of XA instead of 61.8% is not a valid Gartley. Use harmonic scanning tools (available in most charting platforms) to measure ratios automatically, and only trade patterns that fall within the accepted tolerance of each ratio (typically ±2–3%).

3.8 Wyckoff Method

Richard D. Wyckoff (1873–1934) was one of the most influential figures in technical analysis. His method, developed in the early 20th century, is built on the premise that the market is driven by the actions of large institutional operators — what Wyckoff called the Composite Man. By studying price and volume together, traders can identify when these large players are accumulating (buying) or distributing (selling), and position themselves accordingly.

The Wyckoff Method remains essential today because it explains why price moves the way it does. While most retail traders react to price, Wyckoff traders anticipate moves by reading the footprints that institutions leave in the market.

The Composite Man Theory

Wyckoff advised traders to imagine the market as being controlled by a single entity — the Composite Man — who methodically accumulates shares at low prices, marks them up, distributes them to the public at high prices, and then marks them down. This is not a conspiracy; it is simply how large capital operates. Institutions cannot buy or sell millions of shares at once without moving the market, so they must operate in phases over time.

Your job as a trader is to identify what the Composite Man is doing and trade alongside him, not against him. If he is accumulating, you should be buying. If he is distributing, you should be selling or staying flat.

The Wyckoff Accumulation Schematic

Accumulation occurs at the bottom of a downtrend, where institutions are quietly building large positions. The process unfolds through distinct phases, each with characteristic price and volume behavior:

Accumulation Phases

  • Phase A — Stopping the Downtrend: Begins with Preliminary Support (PS) where significant buying first appears after a prolonged decline, slowing momentum. The Selling Climax (SC) follows — a dramatic, high-volume sell-off that exhausts remaining sellers and marks the low of the range. The Automatic Rally (AR) is the reflex bounce that follows the SC, establishing the upper boundary of the trading range. The Secondary Test (ST) revisits the SC area on lower volume, confirming that selling pressure has been absorbed.
  • Phase B — Building a Cause: The longest phase, where the Composite Man is quietly accumulating shares within the trading range. Price oscillates between the SC low and AR high. Volume analysis during this phase reveals whether supply is being absorbed (upswings on higher volume) or still present (downswings on higher volume). Multiple STs may occur.
  • Phase C — The Spring: The most critical phase. The Spring (or shakeout) is a deliberate move below the support established by the SC, designed to trigger stop losses and shake out remaining weak holders. This is where the Composite Man makes his final purchases at the lowest possible prices. The Spring occurs on relatively low volume (indicating lack of genuine selling pressure), and price quickly recovers back into the range. This is often the best entry point.
  • Phase D — Markup Begins: The Sign of Strength (SOS) is a strong rally on increased volume that breaks above the trading range resistance. The Last Point of Support (LPS) is a pullback to the breakout area on reduced volume, confirming that former resistance has become support. This provides a secondary entry point for traders who missed the Spring.
  • Phase E — Uptrend: Price leaves the accumulation range and enters a sustained uptrend. The “cause” built during accumulation now produces the “effect” — the larger the accumulation range (in time and price), the larger the subsequent markup.
Wyckoff accumulation schematic showing the transition from downtrend to accumulation range to markup. The Spring (Phase C) is the key shakeout event where smart money completes its buying.

The Wyckoff Distribution Schematic

Distribution is the mirror image of accumulation, occurring at the top of an uptrend. The Composite Man is selling his accumulated shares to eager retail buyers:

Distribution Phases

  • Phase A — Stopping the Uptrend: Preliminary Supply (PSY) appears as the first significant selling after a prolonged advance. The Buying Climax (BC) is a surge of euphoric buying on high volume that marks the high. The Automatic Reaction (AR) is the pullback that follows, establishing the lower boundary. The Secondary Test (ST) retests the BC area on lower volume.
  • Phase B — Building a Cause: The Composite Man distributes his position within the range. Price oscillates between BC high and AR low. Upswings on decreasing volume and downswings on increasing volume signal distribution.
  • Phase C — The Upthrust (UTAD): The mirror of the Spring. An Upthrust After Distribution (UTAD) is a brief push above resistance to trigger breakout buyers’ entries and short sellers’ stop losses. Price quickly falls back into the range, trapping late buyers.
  • Phase D — Markdown Begins: The Sign of Weakness (SOW) is a sharp decline on heavy volume that breaks below the trading range. The Last Point of Supply (LPSY) is a weak bounce that fails to reach the old range, confirming distribution is complete.
  • Phase E — Downtrend: Price enters a sustained decline as the full effect of distribution unfolds.
Volume Is the Key to Wyckoff

The entire Wyckoff Method revolves around the relationship between price and volume. During accumulation, look for decreasing volume on tests of support (supply is drying up) and increasing volume on rallies (demand is returning). During distribution, the opposite applies. Without volume analysis, Wyckoff schematics are just price patterns — it is volume that reveals institutional intent.

Wyckoff’s Three Laws

1. Supply and Demand: When demand exceeds supply, price rises; when supply exceeds demand, price falls. 2. Cause and Effect: The size of the accumulation or distribution range (the cause) determines the magnitude of the subsequent trend (the effect). 3. Effort vs. Result: If significant volume (effort) produces little price movement (result), a reversal is likely imminent.

3.9 Market Structure & Liquidity

Modern price action analysis has evolved beyond traditional chart patterns into a framework known as Smart Money Concepts (SMC). This approach, heavily influenced by the Wyckoff Method and institutional order flow theory, focuses on understanding where large players place their orders and how they engineer price moves to fill those orders. At its core is the concept of liquidity — the fuel that moves markets.

Liquidity Pools

In any market, orders cluster at predictable locations. Stop losses are placed just above swing highs (for short sellers) and just below swing lows (for long holders). Limit orders stack at round numbers and obvious support/resistance levels. These clusters of resting orders form liquidity pools.

Institutions need liquidity to fill their large orders. A hedge fund wanting to buy 500,000 shares cannot simply place a market order — it would move price dramatically against them. Instead, they need sellers, and sellers are most abundant where stop losses are being triggered. This is why price is often drawn to areas of concentrated liquidity before reversing.

Liquidity Sweeps (Stop Hunts)

A liquidity sweep (also called a stop hunt or liquidity grab) occurs when price briefly pierces a significant level — such as a swing high or swing low — to trigger the cluster of stop losses resting beyond it, then quickly reverses. This is not random market noise; it is a deliberate mechanism through which large players obtain the liquidity they need to fill their positions.

When price sweeps below a swing low:

  • Long holders’ stop losses are triggered, creating a flood of sell orders
  • Breakout short sellers enter, adding more sell orders
  • Institutions use this selling pressure as the counterparty to their buy orders
  • With their orders filled, the buying pressure pushes price back above the level and into a reversal

This is why you so often see “false breakouts” at key levels. They are not false — they are engineered liquidity events.

A bearish-to-bullish liquidity sweep. Price takes out the swing low support, triggering stop losses, before reversing sharply higher as institutions fill buy orders using the triggered sell orders.

Smart Money Concepts (SMC)

Smart Money Concepts is a modern framework that codifies how institutional order flow creates specific price structures. Here are the four essential SMC concepts:

Order Blocks (OB)

An Order Block is the last opposing candle before a strong impulsive move. For a bullish order block, it is the last bearish (red) candle before a sharp rally. For a bearish order block, it is the last bullish (green) candle before a sharp decline.

Order blocks represent price zones where institutions placed their orders. When price returns to an order block, it often finds support or resistance because:

  • Institutions may have unfilled orders remaining at that level
  • The institutional traders who initiated the move may defend their positions
  • The zone represents a price level validated by significant capital commitment

The best order blocks are those that created a break of structure (see below) and have not yet been revisited (“unmitigated”).

Fair Value Gaps (FVG)

A Fair Value Gap (also called an imbalance) is a three-candle pattern where the wicks of the first and third candles do not overlap, leaving a gap in price. This gap represents an area where price moved so aggressively that buy and sell orders were not matched — creating an inefficiency in the market.

Markets tend to fill FVGs over time because:

  • Unfilled orders remain in the gap zone, acting as a magnet for price
  • Algorithmic trading systems are programmed to trade these inefficiencies
  • The gap represents “unfair” pricing that the market seeks to rebalance

Traders use FVGs as entry zones: wait for price to retrace into the gap, then enter in the direction of the original impulsive move. FVGs that align with order blocks are particularly powerful.

Break of Structure (BOS)

A Break of Structure confirms the continuation of the current trend. In an uptrend, price forms higher highs and higher lows. A BOS occurs when price breaks above the most recent swing high, confirming that bulls remain in control and the uptrend structure is intact.

  • Bullish BOS: Price breaks above the previous swing high → uptrend continuation confirmed
  • Bearish BOS: Price breaks below the previous swing low → downtrend continuation confirmed

BOS is used to confirm that the market structure (trend) is still valid. As long as BOS events continue in one direction, traders should look for entries aligned with that trend.

Change of Character (CHOCH)

A Change of Character is the first sign that a trend may be reversing. It occurs when price violates the current market structure for the first time — specifically, when price in an uptrend breaks below the most recent swing low (or in a downtrend, breaks above the most recent swing high).

  • Bearish CHOCH: In an uptrend, price fails to make a higher high and instead breaks below the previous swing low → potential shift from bullish to bearish
  • Bullish CHOCH: In a downtrend, price fails to make a lower low and instead breaks above the previous swing high → potential shift from bearish to bullish

CHOCH is an early warning, not confirmation. After a CHOCH, traders look for a BOS in the new direction to confirm the trend reversal. Combining CHOCH with liquidity sweeps and order blocks greatly increases the probability of catching genuine reversals.

Combining SMC with Wyckoff

Smart Money Concepts and Wyckoff analysis are deeply complementary. A Wyckoff Spring is essentially a liquidity sweep of the accumulation range support. The Sign of Strength is a BOS to the upside. Order blocks form at the points where institutional buying began. When you can identify both the Wyckoff phase and the SMC structure, you have a comprehensive understanding of institutional intent and precise entry zones.

Beware of Hindsight Bias

SMC concepts are extremely easy to identify after the fact but much harder to trade in real time. Not every order block holds, not every FVG gets filled, and not every CHOCH leads to a reversal. Always use risk management (covered in Module 6) and confirmation before entering trades based on these concepts. The best SMC setups occur when multiple concepts align: a liquidity sweep into an order block containing an FVG, followed by a CHOCH on the lower timeframe.
