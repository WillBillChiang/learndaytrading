MODULE 03

Chart Patterns & Structure

Every price chart tells a story. Learning to read market structure through support, resistance, trendlines, and classic chart patterns is the foundation of technical analysis. These patterns repeat because they reflect the collective psychology of buyers and sellers — fear, greed, indecision, and conviction.

3.1 Support & Resistance

Support and resistance are the most fundamental concepts in chart analysis. They represent price levels where buying or selling pressure has historically been strong enough to halt or reverse price movement.

What Is Support?

Support is a price level where demand is strong enough to prevent the price from declining further. Think of it as a "floor" that price bounces off. When price approaches support, buyers step in because they perceive the asset as cheap or undervalued at that level.

What Is Resistance?

Resistance is a price level where selling pressure is strong enough to prevent the price from rising further. It acts as a "ceiling." Sellers emerge at resistance because they perceive the asset as expensive or they are taking profits from earlier entries.

Price repeatedly bounces off support (green) and rejects from resistance (red)

What Makes a Strong S/R Level?

Key Factors for S/R Strength

  • Number of touches: The more times price bounces off a level, the stronger it becomes. Three or more touches confirm significance.
  • Recency: Recent levels matter more than levels from months or years ago. Markets have short memories.
  • Volume at the level: High volume at a support or resistance level indicates strong conviction from participants.
  • Time frame: S/R on higher time frames (daily, weekly) is more significant than on lower time frames (1-min, 5-min).
  • Round numbers: Psychological price levels like $100, $50, or $200 often act as natural support/resistance because traders place orders there.
  • Role reversal: When support breaks, it often becomes resistance (and vice versa). This is one of the most powerful concepts in trading.
Pro Tip: Think in Zones, Not Lines

Support and resistance are rarely exact price points. They are zones or areas. A support zone at $50 might extend from $49.50 to $50.50. Drawing them as thin zones rather than precise lines will make your analysis more realistic and reduce false breakout frustration.

How to Identify S/R Levels

  • Look for areas where price has reversed direction multiple times
  • Identify swing highs and swing lows — these naturally form resistance and support
  • Note where large volume clusters appear on a volume profile
  • Watch for gap zones — unfilled gaps often act as support or resistance
  • Use previous day’s high, low, and close as intraday S/R levels

3.2 Trendlines & Channels

While horizontal S/R captures where price stalls, trendlines capture the direction and pace of the current move. A trendline connects a series of higher lows (in an uptrend) or lower highs (in a downtrend), creating a diagonal line that guides price action.

Uptrend Trendline

An uptrend trendline is drawn by connecting two or more higher lows. The line slopes upward from left to right and acts as dynamic support. As long as price remains above the trendline, the uptrend is intact.

Downtrend Trendline

A downtrend trendline connects two or more lower highs. The line slopes downward and acts as dynamic resistance. Price breaking above a downtrend trendline signals a potential trend reversal.

Ascending channel (left) acts as dynamic support; Descending channel (right) acts as dynamic resistance

Trendline Rules

Drawing Valid Trendlines

  • Minimum 3 touches: Two points define a line; the third touch confirms it. More touches = stronger trendline.
  • Steeper = weaker: Very steep trendlines (above 60°) are unsustainable and break quickly. The most reliable trendlines have a moderate slope around 30–45°.
  • Respect the wicks: Generally, connect the wicks (highs/lows) rather than candle bodies for more accurate trendlines.
  • Adjust, don’t force: If price slightly violates a trendline but recovers, it may be worth redrawing rather than declaring it broken.
  • Volume on break: A trendline break accompanied by above-average volume is far more significant than a low-volume violation.
Common Mistake: Over-drawing Trendlines

New traders often force trendlines onto charts to see what they want to see. If you have to "squint" to see the trendline, it probably does not exist. The best trendlines are obvious — anyone looking at the chart should see them.

3.3 Classic Chart Patterns — Reversal

Reversal patterns signal that the current trend is losing momentum and is likely to reverse direction. These patterns develop over time and are most reliable when they appear after a sustained trend. Let us examine the major reversal patterns every day trader should know.

Head & Shoulders Bearish

  • Structure: Three peaks — the middle (head) is the highest, flanked by two lower, roughly equal peaks (shoulders).
  • Neckline: Connect the lows between the left shoulder/head and head/right shoulder. This is your trigger line.
  • Entry: Short when price breaks below the neckline on increased volume.
  • Stop loss: Above the right shoulder.
  • Price target: Measure the distance from the head to the neckline; project that same distance downward from the breakout point.
  • Volume: Volume typically decreases from left shoulder to head to right shoulder, then surges on the neckline break.

Inverse Head & Shoulders Bullish

  • Structure: The mirror image — three troughs, with the middle (head) being the lowest.
  • Entry: Go long when price breaks above the neckline.
  • Stop loss: Below the right shoulder.
  • Price target: Distance from head to neckline projected upward.
  • Volume: Often increases dramatically on the breakout above the neckline.

Double Top Bearish

  • Structure: Two peaks at roughly the same price level, forming an "M" shape.
  • Signal: Bearish — indicates buyers could not push above resistance twice, and momentum is fading.
  • Entry: Short on break below the neckline (the valley between the two peaks).
  • Stop loss: Above the peaks.
  • Price target: Height of the pattern (peak to valley) projected downward from the break.

Double Bottom Bullish

  • Structure: Two troughs at roughly the same price level, forming a "W" shape.
  • Signal: Bullish — sellers could not push price below support twice, indicating a shift to buying.
  • Entry: Long on break above the neckline (the peak between the troughs).
  • Stop loss: Below the troughs.
  • Price target: Height of the pattern projected upward from the breakout point.

Triple Top / Triple Bottom Reversal

  • Structure: Three peaks (triple top) or three troughs (triple bottom) at roughly the same level.
  • Rarer but stronger: Triple tops/bottoms are less common than doubles but signal even stronger reversal conviction.
  • Entry: Same as double top/bottom — on break of the neckline after the third touch.
  • Volume: Typically diminishes on each successive peak/trough and surges on the breakout.
Context Matters

Reversal patterns are only valid when they appear after a sustained trend. A head-and-shoulders that forms in a sideways range carries far less significance than one that caps a multi-week uptrend. Always consider the bigger picture.

3.4 Classic Chart Patterns — Continuation

Continuation patterns form during a pause in the current trend, before price resumes in the same direction. Think of them as the market "catching its breath" before the next leg. These patterns are your opportunity to enter a trending move.

Bull Flag Bullish

  • Structure: A sharp upward move (flagpole) followed by a gentle downward-sloping consolidation channel (flag).
  • Entry: Buy when price breaks above the upper boundary of the flag.
  • Stop loss: Below the lowest point of the flag.
  • Price target: Length of the flagpole projected upward from the breakout.
  • Volume: High volume on the flagpole, declining during the flag, then surging on breakout.

Bear Flag Bearish

  • Structure: A sharp drop (flagpole) followed by a gentle upward-drifting consolidation (flag).
  • Entry: Short when price breaks below the lower boundary of the flag.
  • Price target: Length of the flagpole projected downward.

Pennant Continuation

  • Structure: Like a flag, but the consolidation forms a small symmetrical triangle (converging trendlines) instead of a channel.
  • Duration: Typically short — a few days to two weeks. Longer formations become symmetrical triangles.
  • Entry & target: Same as a flag pattern — break of the pennant boundary with target based on the flagpole length.

Triangles Multiple Types

Triangle Type Flat Side Sloping Side Bias
Ascending Horizontal resistance (top) Rising support (bottom) Bullish
Descending Horizontal support (bottom) Declining resistance (top) Bearish
Symmetric Neither Both converge equally Neutral

Wedges Reversal / Continuation

  • Rising wedge: Both trendlines slope upward, but the lower line is steeper. Looks bullish but is actually bearish — it signals fading upward momentum. Price usually breaks down.
  • Falling wedge: Both trendlines slope downward, but the upper line is steeper. Looks bearish but is actually bullish — selling pressure is exhausting. Price usually breaks up.
  • Key difference from triangles: In wedges, both lines slope in the same direction. In triangles, at least one line is horizontal or they slope in opposite directions.

3.5 Volume Confirmation

Price tells you what happened; volume tells you how strongly the market feels about it. No chart pattern analysis is complete without examining volume. Volume acts as the lie detector of technical analysis — it validates or invalidates every pattern you see.

Volume Rules for Chart Patterns

Scenario Volume Behavior Interpretation
Breakout from resistance High volume surge (1.5x+ average) Strong — Likely genuine breakout
Breakout on low volume Below average Weak — High probability of a fakeout / false breakout
During consolidation pattern Declining / contracting Normal — Expected; coiling = potential energy building
Trend continuation Volume in direction of trend Healthy — Trend has conviction
Price rising, volume falling Divergence Caution — Rally may be running out of steam
Support break with volume High volume on breakdown Bearish — Serious breakdown, sellers in control
The Breakout Volume Rule

When a chart pattern breaks out, volume should ideally be at least 1.5 to 2 times the recent average. This single confirmation filter can save you from dozens of false breakout trades per month. If volume is not there, sit on your hands and wait.

Volume Divergence

One of the most powerful warning signals is volume divergence — when price makes new highs but volume is declining. This suggests fewer participants are supporting the move, and a reversal may be imminent. Conversely, price making new lows on declining volume can signal a bottom is forming.

Don’t Trade Patterns in Isolation

A perfect-looking head and shoulders with declining volume on the breakdown is likely a false signal. Always combine chart patterns with volume analysis and, ideally, indicator confirmation (covered in Module 4) to increase your probability of success.
