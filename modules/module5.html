MODULE 05

Risk Management

The most critical skill in trading — protecting your capital so you survive long enough to profit.

5.1 Why Risk Management Is Everything

Here is a sobering reality: roughly 90% of day traders lose money. The number one reason? Not bad strategies or poor timing — it is catastrophic, unmanaged losses that wipe them out before they ever get a chance to learn.

Risk management is not the sexy part of trading. It is the part that keeps you alive. A mediocre strategy with excellent risk management will outperform a brilliant strategy with no risk management every single time.

The Math of Ruin

Losses and gains are not symmetrical. A 50% loss does not require a 50% gain to recover — it requires a 100% gain. The deeper you dig the hole, the exponentially harder it becomes to climb out. This asymmetry is the single most important concept in trading.

The Drawdown Recovery Table

Study this table carefully. It should change how you think about every single trade:

% Loss of Capital % Gain Needed to Recover Difficulty
5% 5.3% Easy
10% 11.1% Manageable
20% 25.0% Challenging
25% 33.3% Difficult
33% 50.0% Very Difficult
50% 100.0% Extremely Hard
75% 300.0% Nearly Impossible
90% 900.0% Game Over
The Point of No Return

Once you lose 50% of your account, you need to double your remaining money just to break even. At a 75% drawdown, you need a 300% return. Most traders who reach this point never recover. This is why preventing large drawdowns is the absolute foundation of professional trading.

The message is clear: small losses are recoverable; large losses are career-ending. Every rule in this module exists to keep your losses small.

5.2 The 1% Rule

The 1% Rule is the cornerstone of professional risk management. It is elegant in its simplicity:

The 1% Rule

Never risk more than 1-2% of your total account on any single trade.

Risk Amount = Account Size × Risk Percentage

Why 1-2%?

  • A streak of 10 consecutive losses (which will happen) only costs you 10-20% of your account — painful but recoverable.
  • It prevents emotional decision-making because no single trade can seriously damage you.
  • It gives you hundreds of "at bats" to learn and improve before running out of capital.
  • Professional hedge funds and prop firms use similar or even stricter limits.

Math Examples

Account Size Risk % Max Risk Per Trade 10-Loss Streak Impact
$10,000 1% $100 -$1,000 (10%)
$25,000 1% $250 -$2,500 (10%)
$25,000 2% $500 -$5,000 (20%)
$50,000 1% $500 -$5,000 (10%)
$100,000 0.5% $500 -$5,000 (5%)
Pro Tip: Start with 0.5%

When you are just starting out, consider risking only 0.5% per trade. This gives you a much larger margin of safety while you are still learning. You can always increase your risk percentage as your skills and confidence develop.

5.3 Position Sizing

Position sizing is the bridge between your risk rule and the actual trade. It tells you exactly how many shares to buy based on your risk tolerance and your stop loss distance.

The Position Sizing Formula

Shares = Risk Amount ÷ (Entry Price − Stop Loss Price)

This formula guarantees that if your stop loss is hit, you lose exactly your predetermined risk amount — no more, no less.

Example 1: Standard Setup

Scenario: Buying AAPL at $175

Account: $25,000 | Risk: 1% ($250) | Entry: $175.00 | Stop: $173.00
Risk per share: $175 - $173 = $2.00
Shares = $250 ÷ $2.00 = 125 shares
Position value: 125 × $175 = $21,875 | If stopped out, you lose exactly $250.

Example 2: Tight Stop

Scenario: Buying TSLA at $250

Account: $25,000 | Risk: 1% ($250) | Entry: $250.00 | Stop: $249.00
Risk per share: $250 - $249 = $1.00
Shares = $250 ÷ $1.00 = 250 shares
Position value: 250 × $250 = $62,500 (requires margin). The tighter the stop, the larger the position.

Example 3: Wide Stop

Scenario: Buying NVDA at $800

Account: $25,000 | Risk: 1% ($250) | Entry: $800.00 | Stop: $790.00
Risk per share: $800 - $790 = $10.00
Shares = $250 ÷ $10.00 = 25 shares
Position value: 25 × $800 = $20,000. A wider stop means a smaller position to keep risk constant.

Never Adjust the Stop to Fit the Position

A common beginner mistake: wanting a bigger position so they move the stop closer. This defeats the entire purpose. Your stop loss should be placed at a technically meaningful level. Then your position size adjusts to accommodate the stop — never the other way around.

ATR-Based Volatility-Adjusted Position Sizing

The basic position sizing formula uses a fixed-dollar stop distance. But a $2 stop on a stock with an Average True Range (ATR) of $1 is very different from a $2 stop on a stock with an ATR of $5. ATR-based position sizing automatically adjusts your share count based on the stock’s current volatility, ensuring each trade carries approximately the same risk in volatility-adjusted terms.

Formula: Position Size = Account Risk $ / (N × ATR)

Where N is your ATR multiplier (typically 1.5–3x depending on your strategy’s holding period and timeframe).

Worked Example: $50,000 account, 1% risk ($500), stock ATR(14) = $2.50, using 2x ATR stop:

  • Stop distance = 2 × $2.50 = $5.00
  • Position size = $500 / $5.00 = 100 shares
  • If the same stock’s ATR rises to $5.00 (double the volatility): Stop = 2 × $5.00 = $10.00, Position = $500 / $10.00 = 50 shares

Notice how the formula automatically halves your position size when volatility doubles. This is the power of volatility-adjusted sizing — it protects you in volatile markets and allows larger positions in calm markets.

Market Condition Typical ATR Fixed $2 Stop (Shares) ATR-Based Stop (Shares) Which Is Safer?
Calm (low vol) $1.00 250 shares 250 shares (2x$1 stop) Equivalent
Normal $2.50 250 shares 100 shares (2x$2.50 stop) ATR-based — fixed stop is too tight
Volatile (earnings, FOMC) $5.00 250 shares 50 shares (2x$5 stop) ATR-based — fixed stop guarantees being stopped out
Extreme (crash/squeeze) $10.00 250 shares 25 shares (2x$10 stop) ATR-based — fixed stop is suicidal

Slippage Modeling in Position Sizing

Every position sizing formula assumes you get filled at your exact stop price. In reality, you often don’t. Slippage — the difference between your intended fill price and your actual fill price — erodes your risk management. Fast-moving markets, low-liquidity stocks, and stop-market orders are the biggest culprits.

Practical rule: Add a slippage buffer of 0.5–1.0x ATR to your stop distance when calculating position size. This ensures your actual risk stays within your target even if slippage occurs.

Example: If your ATR stop is $5.00 and you add a 0.5 ATR slippage buffer ($1.25), your effective stop distance becomes $6.25. Position size = $500 / $6.25 = 80 shares instead of 100. This small adjustment can prevent the frustrating reality of losing 1.5% on a trade that was supposed to risk only 1%.

  • Liquid large-caps (AAPL, MSFT, SPY): Use 0.25–0.5 ATR slippage buffer
  • Mid-cap stocks ($1B–$10B market cap): Use 0.5–0.75 ATR slippage buffer
  • Small-cap / low-float stocks: Use 1.0+ ATR slippage buffer — or consider using limit stops instead of market stops

5.5 Stop Loss Strategies

A stop loss is your emergency exit — a predetermined price at which you close a losing trade. There are several approaches, each with trade-offs. Understanding them lets you choose the right tool for each setup.

Fixed Dollar Stop

Set a fixed dollar amount you are willing to lose (e.g., always risk $200).

  • Pros: Simple, consistent, easy to track
  • Cons: Ignores chart structure; stop may land at an illogical price
  • Best for: Beginners who need a hard rule

Percentage Stop

Place the stop a fixed percentage below entry (e.g., 2% below entry).

  • Pros: Scales with price, straightforward calculation
  • Cons: Still arbitrary; does not account for volatility or support levels
  • Best for: Swing traders, position traders

ATR-Based Stop

Use Average True Range to set stops based on actual volatility (e.g., 1.5× ATR below entry).

  • Pros: Adapts to volatility; avoids getting stopped out by normal noise
  • Cons: Requires ATR calculation; can be wide in volatile stocks
  • Best for: Experienced traders, volatile stocks

Technical / Structure Stop

Place the stop below a key support level, moving average, or chart structure.

  • Pros: Most logical placement; based on where the trade idea is invalid
  • Cons: Requires chart reading skill; stop distance varies
  • Best for: Day traders, technical traders (recommended)
The Best Approach: Combine Methods

Professional traders typically place stops at technical levels and then use position sizing to ensure the dollar risk stays within their 1-2% rule. This gives you the best of both worlds: a logically placed stop and controlled risk.

Never Trade Without a Stop Loss

"I will just watch it and exit manually" is a plan that fails under emotional pressure. Always have your stop entered in the market before the trade moves. Hope is not a risk management strategy.

5.6 Risk/Reward Ratios

The risk/reward ratio (R:R) compares how much you stand to lose versus how much you stand to gain on a trade. It is the second pillar of risk management, alongside position sizing.

Risk/Reward Ratio

R:R = (Target Price − Entry Price) ÷ (Entry Price − Stop Loss Price)

A 1:2 R:R means you risk $1 to make $2. A 1:3 R:R means you risk $1 to make $3. Always aim for a minimum of 1:2.

Why R:R Trumps Win Rate

Most beginners obsess over win rate. But the math shows that a trader with a modest win rate and a high R:R ratio crushes a trader with a high win rate and poor R:R. Here is the proof:

Win Rate R:R Ratio Result per 100 Trades ($100 risk each) Outcome
60% 1:1 60 × $100 − 40 × $100 = +$2,000 Profitable
50% 1:2 50 × $200 − 50 × $100 = +$5,000 Very Profitable
40% 1:3 40 × $300 − 60 × $100 = +$6,000 Highly Profitable
30% 1:4 30 × $400 − 70 × $100 = +$5,000 Very Profitable
70% 1:0.5 70 × $50 − 30 × $100 = +$500 Barely Profitable
50% 1:1 50 × $100 − 50 × $100 = $0 Break Even
40% 1:1 40 × $100 − 60 × $100 = −$2,000 Losing
The 40% Winner

Look at the 40% win rate with 1:3 R:R. This trader loses more often than they win, yet they make $6,000 per 100 trades. You do not need to be right most of the time to be very profitable. You just need your winners to be significantly bigger than your losers.

The Expectancy Formula

Trade Expectancy

Expectancy = (Win Rate × Avg Win) − (Loss Rate × Avg Loss)

If your expectancy is positive, you have an edge. Every trade you take moves you closer to long-term profitability, regardless of individual outcomes.

5.8 Maximum Daily Loss Rules

Individual trade risk limits are necessary but not sufficient. You also need a circuit breaker for the entire trading day. This is your maximum daily loss rule.

The Daily Loss Limit

Set a maximum daily loss of 2-3% of your account. When you hit this limit, you stop trading for the day. No exceptions. No "one more trade to make it back."

Why This Rule Exists

  • Tilt prevention: After multiple losses, your judgment deteriorates. Emotional trading leads to bigger losses, which leads to more emotional trading — a destructive spiral.
  • Capital preservation: A 3% daily loss limit means even five terrible days in a row only costs you 15% — still very recoverable.
  • Forced objectivity: Stepping away gives you time to review what went wrong with a clear head.
  • Consistency: It prevents catastrophic "blow-up" days that can erase weeks of profits.

Implementing the Rule

Account Size Daily Loss Limit (3%) Max Individual Trades (at 1% risk)
$10,000 $300 3 full losses before stopping
$25,000 $750 3 full losses before stopping
$50,000 $1,500 3 full losses before stopping
$100,000 $3,000 3 full losses before stopping
The Hardest Rule to Follow

This is arguably the hardest discipline in trading. After hitting your daily limit, every fiber of your being will scream "just one more trade to make it back." That impulse is exactly why the rule exists. The market will be there tomorrow. Your capital might not be if you keep trading on tilt.

What to Do After Hitting Your Limit

Step away from the screen. Go for a walk. Journal your trades. Review your decisions. Ask yourself: were the setups good and you were just unlucky? Or were you forcing trades? This reflection is often more valuable than the trading itself.

5.9 The Kelly Criterion

So far we have discussed risking a fixed percentage per trade — typically 1%. But is there a mathematically optimal amount to risk? The answer is yes, and it was discovered by a Bell Labs scientist named John Kelly Jr. in 1956. The formula was later popularized for financial markets by Ed Thorp, a mathematics professor who first used it to beat blackjack (documented in his legendary book Beat the Dealer) and then applied it to Wall Street with extraordinary success.

The Kelly Criterion tells you the optimal fraction of your capital to risk on each bet to maximize the long-term geometric growth rate of your wealth. It is the mathematically proven sweet spot between risking too little (leaving money on the table) and risking too much (blowing up).

The Kelly Formula

The basic Kelly formula for trading is:

Kelly % = (bp − q) / b

Where:

  • b = the ratio of average win to average loss (your reward-to-risk ratio)
  • p = the probability of winning (your win rate)
  • q = the probability of losing (1 − p)

Alternatively, this can be written as: Kelly % = p − (q / b) which some traders find more intuitive. Both formulas produce the same result.

Worked Example

Suppose your trading system has these statistics based on 200+ trades:

  • Win rate (p): 55% = 0.55
  • Loss rate (q): 45% = 0.45
  • Average win / Average loss (b): 1.5 (you make $1.50 for every $1.00 you lose)

Kelly % = (1.5 × 0.55 − 0.45) / 1.5 = (0.825 − 0.45) / 1.5 = 0.375 / 1.5 = 0.25 = 25%

Full Kelly says to risk 25% of your account per trade. That sounds insane — and in practice, it is.

Why Full Kelly Is Too Aggressive

The Kelly Criterion is mathematically optimal for maximizing long-term growth, but it assumes you know your exact edge with perfect precision. In reality:

  • Your statistics are estimated, not known: A 55% win rate based on 200 trades has significant statistical uncertainty. Your true win rate might be 50% or 60%.
  • Markets change: The edge you measured last year may not exist next month. Regime changes can destroy strategies overnight.
  • Drawdowns are brutal: Full Kelly produces equity swings of 50-80% drawdowns even when you have a genuine edge. Very few humans can psychologically endure this.
  • It assumes infinite time horizon: Kelly maximizes terminal wealth over an infinite number of bets. You do not have infinite time or patience.

As Ralph Vince demonstrated in Portfolio Management Formulas, even a slight overestimation of your edge when using full Kelly can turn a winning system into a losing one through over-betting. The penalty for over-betting is far more severe than the penalty for under-betting.

Full Kelly Is a Theoretical Ceiling, Not a Target

Never use full Kelly in live trading. Even Ed Thorp himself — the man who popularized the formula — has said he rarely risked more than half Kelly. Full Kelly is the absolute maximum you should ever consider, and in practice you should stay well below it.

Fractional Kelly: The Practical Solution

Professional traders and fund managers use fractional Kelly — risking only a fraction of what the full Kelly formula recommends. This dramatically reduces volatility while sacrificing only a modest amount of return.

Kelly Fraction Risk per Trade (Example) Expected Growth Max Drawdown Range Practical Assessment
Full Kelly (100%) 25.0% Maximum (100%) 50–85% Reckless — Do Not Use
Three-Quarter Kelly (75%) 18.75% ~94% of max 40–65% Still Too Aggressive
Half Kelly (50%) 12.5% ~75% of max 25–40% Aggressive but Viable
Quarter Kelly (25%) 6.25% ~50% of max 10–20% Conservative & Recommended
Tenth Kelly (10%) 2.5% ~25% of max 5–10% Very Safe
The Sweet Spot: Quarter to Half Kelly

Most professional traders and quantitative fund managers use between quarter Kelly and half Kelly. At half Kelly, you capture roughly 75% of the theoretical maximum growth rate while cutting your worst-case drawdowns nearly in half. At quarter Kelly, you capture about 50% of maximum growth with very manageable drawdowns. For day traders, quarter Kelly (which often works out to roughly 1–3% risk per trade) aligns perfectly with the 1% rule discussed earlier in this module.

Key Takeaways on the Kelly Criterion

  • Calculate your Kelly percentage using your actual trading statistics (minimum 100+ trades for meaningful data).
  • Never use full Kelly. Use quarter Kelly or half Kelly at most.
  • Recalculate regularly as your statistics evolve over time.
  • When in doubt, bet smaller. The penalty for under-betting is linear; the penalty for over-betting is catastrophic.

5.10 Monte Carlo Simulation

You have backtested your strategy and it looks profitable. Your win rate is solid, your risk/reward is good, and the equity curve goes up. But here is the uncomfortable truth: that backtest shows you only one possible path out of thousands. What if your losing trades had clustered differently? What if your biggest winners came at different times? Would you still have survived?

Monte Carlo simulation answers these questions by taking your actual historical trade results and randomly reshuffling them thousands of times, generating thousands of alternative equity curves. Each curve represents one possible sequence of trades you could have experienced. The result is not a single equity curve but a probability distribution of outcomes.

How Monte Carlo Simulation Works for Traders

The process is straightforward:

  1. Collect your trade results: A list of all your individual trade P&L values (e.g., +$150, -$100, +$200, -$95, …).
  2. Randomly resample with replacement: Draw trades at random from your list to create a new simulated sequence of the same length. Some trades will appear multiple times; others will be skipped.
  3. Build a simulated equity curve: Starting from your account balance, apply each randomly drawn trade sequentially.
  4. Repeat 1,000–10,000 times: Each repetition produces a different equity curve with a different drawdown profile and different final result.
  5. Analyze the distribution: Look at the median result, the 5th percentile (worst realistic case), the 95th percentile (best realistic case), and maximum drawdown across all simulations.

What Monte Carlo Simulation Reveals

  • Probability of various drawdown levels: Instead of seeing the one drawdown from your backtest, you see the range of drawdowns you might experience. Maybe your backtest had a 15% max drawdown, but the simulation reveals a 10% chance of a 30%+ drawdown.
  • Confidence intervals for returns: Rather than "my strategy returns 40% per year," you learn "there is a 90% probability my strategy returns between 15% and 65% per year."
  • Statistical significance of your edge: If more than 5–10% of simulations result in a net loss, your edge may not be statistically robust.
  • Ruin probability: The percentage of simulations where your account drops below a critical threshold (e.g., 50% drawdown). This is arguably the single most important output.

Ruin Probability

Ruin probability is the percentage of Monte Carlo simulations in which your account hits a catastrophic drawdown threshold — typically defined as a 50% loss from peak equity. This is the single most critical number from a Monte Carlo analysis.

A robust trading system should have a ruin probability below 1% when tested at your actual position size. If your ruin probability is above 5%, your position sizing is too aggressive, regardless of what your backtest equity curve looks like.

Practical Guidelines

Parameter Recommended Value Why
Number of simulations 1,000 minimum; 10,000 ideal Fewer than 1,000 gives unstable statistics
Focus curve 5th percentile (worst realistic) Plan for the worst, not the average
Ruin threshold 50% drawdown from peak Recovery from 50%+ drawdown is extremely difficult
Acceptable ruin probability Below 1% Even 1% means 1-in-100 chance of catastrophic loss
Minimum trade sample size 100+ trades Fewer trades make the simulation unreliable
Monte Carlo Cannot Predict the Future

Monte Carlo simulation reshuffles your past trades. It assumes your future trades will come from the same statistical distribution. If market conditions change fundamentally (and they will), your actual results may fall outside the simulated range. Use Monte Carlo as a stress test, not a crystal ball.

Free Tools for Monte Carlo

You do not need to code your own simulator. Many free and paid tools exist: Excel/Google Sheets with a random resampling formula, dedicated platforms like Edgewonk and TradeStation, or simple Python scripts. The key is to actually run the simulation rather than assuming your backtest equity curve is the only possible outcome.

Sequence Risk: Why Order Matters

Consider two traders with identical statistics: 60% win rate, 2:1 reward-to-risk ratio, risking 2% per trade. Over 100 trades, both will have approximately 60 wins and 40 losses. But the order in which those wins and losses occur creates dramatically different outcomes for a compounding account.

  • Favorable sequence: Wins clustered early, building a larger base. Losses later are absorbed by accumulated gains. Ending balance: significantly above average.
  • Unfavorable sequence: Losses clustered early, shrinking the base. Even when wins eventually come, they are calculated on a smaller capital base. Ending balance: significantly below average.
  • Same statistics, different results: In a compounding account, a 10-trade losing streak at the beginning of your career is far more damaging than the same streak after 200 profitable trades.

This is the fundamental insight: standard Monte Carlo simulation with independent, identically distributed (IID) resampling understates sequence risk for compounding accounts. Each simulated path treats trades as independent events, but in reality, a string of early losses compounds into a deeper hole than a string of late losses.

Practical implication: Start with smaller position sizes (0.5% risk instead of 1%) until you have a 50+ trade track record demonstrating your edge. This protects against unfavorable early sequences. Only scale up after your account has built a cushion of confirmed profits.

5.11 Correlation Risk & Portfolio Heat

You have learned to risk only 1% per trade. You have learned to set daily loss limits. But there is a subtle and devastating trap that catches even disciplined traders: correlation risk. If you are long AAPL, MSFT, GOOGL, NVDA, and META simultaneously — each risking 1% — you are not taking five independent 1% risks. You are effectively taking one big 5% bet on the tech sector.

When the market drops, correlated positions drop together. A Federal Reserve announcement, a geopolitical shock, or a sector rotation can hit all your positions simultaneously. Your carefully planned 1% risk per trade becomes a 5% portfolio-level disaster in minutes.

What Is Portfolio Heat?

Portfolio heat is the total amount of capital at risk across all your open positions at any given moment. If you have three trades open, each risking 1%, your portfolio heat is 3%. If you have six trades open at 1% risk each, your portfolio heat is 6%.

The critical rule: never let your total portfolio heat exceed 5–6%. This means if you are already in five trades at 1% risk each (5% total heat), you do not open a sixth position until one of the existing trades is closed or moved to breakeven.

Understanding Correlation

Correlation is measured on a scale from −1.0 to +1.0:

  • +1.0 (perfect positive correlation): Two assets move in lockstep. When one goes up, the other always goes up by a proportional amount.
  • 0.0 (no correlation): The movements of the two assets are completely independent.
  • −1.0 (perfect negative correlation): When one goes up, the other always goes down by a proportional amount.

In practice, most stocks within the same sector have correlations between +0.5 and +0.9. This means they frequently move together, especially during market-wide selloffs.

Correlation Goes to 1 in a Crisis

During normal markets, different sectors have moderate correlations. But during a genuine market panic — a flash crash, a financial crisis, a global shock — correlations spike toward 1.0. Everything sells off together. This phenomenon has been observed in every major crisis: 2008, the COVID crash of March 2020, and countless smaller selloffs. Your "diversified" positions become a single concentrated bet precisely when you need diversification the most.

Managing Portfolio Heat and Correlation

Practical Rules for Correlation-Aware Trading

  • Max portfolio heat: Never exceed 5–6% of total account risk across all open positions combined.
  • Sector concentration limit: No more than 2–3 positions in the same sector at the same time.
  • Correlated positions = reduced size: If you have two highly correlated trades (e.g., AAPL and MSFT), risk only 0.5% on each instead of 1% — treat them as a single trade from a risk perspective.
  • Opposite-side hedging: If heavily long tech, consider a short position in a correlated index (like QQQ puts) to offset portfolio-level risk.
  • Count your true bets: Before opening any new position, ask: "If the overall market drops 2% right now, how many of my positions lose money?" If the answer is "all of them," you are not diversified.

Think in Terms of "True Independent Bets"

Professional portfolio managers use the concept of "true independent bets" rather than counting positions. Five tech stocks is roughly one independent bet. Two tech stocks plus an energy stock plus a short on bonds — that might be three independent bets. Always ask yourself: "How many truly uncorrelated risks am I taking?" The answer is usually far fewer than the number of positions on your screen.

Earnings Season Amplifies Correlation Risk

During earnings season, one company's results can move an entire sector. If AAPL misses earnings, MSFT, GOOGL, and the whole tech sector will likely gap down the next morning. Be especially cautious about sector concentration when your holdings are approaching earnings dates.
