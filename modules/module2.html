MODULE 02

Reading Price Action — Candlesticks

Candlestick charts are the language of price action. Every candle tells a story about the battle between buyers and sellers during a specific time period. This module teaches you to read that story fluently — from single-candle signals to powerful multi-candle reversal patterns that professional traders rely on every day.

2.1 Candlestick Anatomy

A candlestick encodes four data points into a single visual shape: the Open, High, Low, and Close (OHLC) for a given time period. Whether you are on a 1-minute chart or a weekly chart, the anatomy is identical.

Bullish vs. Bearish Candle

What the Candle Tells You

  • Long body — Strong conviction. Buyers (green) or sellers (red) dominated the period.
  • Short body — Indecision. Neither side had a clear advantage.
  • Long upper wick — Price was pushed up but sellers rejected higher prices and drove it back down. Bearish signal at resistance.
  • Long lower wick — Price was pushed down but buyers stepped in and drove it back up. Bullish signal at support.
  • No wicks (Marubozu) — Maximum conviction. The open or close is the high or low of the period.
Pro Tip: Context Is Everything

A hammer candlestick at the bottom of a downtrend is a strong reversal signal. The same candle in the middle of a range is meaningless noise. Always read candles in context of the trend, support/resistance, and volume.

2.3 Single Candlestick Patterns

These patterns form on a single candle and can signal reversals, continuations, or indecision. They are the building blocks of price action reading.

Doji

Open and close are nearly identical, creating a cross or plus shape. It signals indecision — the battle between buyers and sellers ended in a draw.

Hammer & Inverted Hammer

Both are bullish reversal signals found at the bottom of downtrends. The long wick shows that sellers pushed the price far in one direction but buyers fought back.

Shooting Star

The bearish mirror of the hammer. Found at the top of uptrends, it signals that buyers pushed prices higher but sellers overwhelmed them by the close.

Marubozu

A candle with no wicks at all — maximum conviction. The open equals the high or low, and the close equals the opposite extreme. These candles represent total dominance by one side.

Spinning Top

A small body with roughly equal upper and lower wicks. Similar to a doji but with a visible body. It indicates indecision and often appears during consolidation.

Bullish Singles

  • Hammer — at bottom of downtrend
  • Inverted Hammer — at bottom of downtrend
  • Bullish Marubozu — strong buying pressure
  • Dragonfly Doji — at support levels

Bearish Singles

  • Shooting Star — at top of uptrend
  • Hanging Man — hammer at top of uptrend
  • Bearish Marubozu — strong selling pressure
  • Gravestone Doji — at resistance levels

2.4 Multi-Candle Patterns

Multi-candle patterns are more reliable than single candles because they show a shift in momentum over multiple periods. These are the patterns that institutional traders and algorithms scan for.

Bullish Engulfing

A large green candle completely "engulfs" the previous red candle's body. Found at the bottom of a downtrend, it signals that buyers have overwhelmed sellers with force. The bigger the engulfing candle relative to the prior candle, the stronger the signal.

Bearish Engulfing

The mirror pattern. A large red candle engulfs the previous green candle at the top of an uptrend. It indicates that sellers have seized control and a reversal may follow.

Morning Star (3-Candle Bullish Reversal)

A three-candle pattern that signals a bottom. First: a large bearish candle (selling continues). Second: a small-bodied candle that gaps down (indecision — selling pressure exhausted). Third: a large bullish candle that closes above the midpoint of the first candle (buyers take over).

Evening Star (3-Candle Bearish Reversal)

The bearish counterpart to the Morning Star. First: a large bullish candle (buying continues). Second: a small-bodied candle that gaps up (indecision at the top). Third: a large bearish candle that closes below the midpoint of the first candle (sellers take control).

Three White Soldiers & Three Black Crows

Three consecutive strong candles in the same direction. These are continuation/confirmation patterns that signal strong momentum.

Warning: Never Trade Patterns in Isolation

A Morning Star on a 5-minute chart means nothing if the daily trend is strongly bearish. Always confirm candlestick patterns with: (1) the higher timeframe trend, (2) support/resistance levels, and (3) volume. A hammer on 10x average volume is far more reliable than a hammer on low volume.

Pro Tip: The Best Patterns Have Confluence

Professional traders look for "confluence" — multiple signals pointing in the same direction. A bullish engulfing at a key support level, with a bounce off the 50-day moving average, on increasing volume? That is a high-probability setup. One signal alone is a coin flip.

2.5 Volume-Confirmed Patterns

The difference between a high-probability candlestick signal and a false one often comes down to a single variable: volume. Professional traders never trade candle patterns without checking volume confirmation.

Why Volume Matters

Volume represents the number of shares (or contracts) traded during a candle’s time period. It answers a critical question: How much conviction is behind this price move?

  • High volume + reversal pattern — Strong signal. Many participants are involved in the reversal, increasing reliability by 40-60% according to Bulkowski's research.
  • Low volume + reversal pattern — Weak signal. Few participants means the "reversal" may just be a lack of interest, not a genuine shift in sentiment.
  • Climactic volume — A massive volume spike (3-5x average) at the end of a trend often marks exhaustion — the last buyers/sellers piling in before the move reverses.
  • Declining volume into a pattern — Volume decreasing as a pattern forms (e.g., during a triangle) signals coiling energy that will release in a breakout.
Pattern Volume Confirmation Reliability Without Volume Reliability With Volume
Hammer Volume > 1.5x average on the hammer candle ~45% ~65%
Engulfing Engulfing candle volume > prior candle volume ~50% ~70%
Morning Star Third candle has highest volume of the three ~55% ~75%
Doji at support Above-average volume shows institutional interest ~35% ~55%
The Volume Spike Rule

When you see a candlestick pattern you like, immediately check: is volume at least 1.5x the 20-period average? If yes, the pattern is worth trading. If volume is below average, pass. This single filter eliminates the majority of false signals.

2.6 Multi-Timeframe Candle Analysis

The most powerful application of candlestick analysis is reading patterns across multiple timeframes simultaneously. A signal that appears on two or three timeframes at once has dramatically higher reliability than a single-timeframe signal.

The Top-Down Approach

Professional traders read candles from higher timeframes to lower timeframes, using each level to confirm or deny the setup:

  • Daily chart — Determines the overall trend and identifies key reversal candles at major support/resistance. This is your directional bias.
  • 15-minute chart — Shows intraday trend structure. Look for candle patterns that align with the daily bias.
  • 5-minute chart — Your execution timeframe. Entry signals should align with 15-min and daily direction.
  • 1-minute chart — Precision timing only. Use for exact entry triggers, not for pattern identification.

Multi-Timeframe Alignment

Advanced: Candle Aggregation Principle

A key insight from multi-timeframe analysis: three 5-minute candles aggregate into one 15-minute candle. This means you can "see" the 15-minute candle forming in real time by watching the 5-minute chart. If three consecutive 5-min bullish candles are forming, the 15-min candle will be a strong bullish marubozu. This gives you an early signal before the 15-min candle closes.

  • 3 green 5-min candles = bullish 15-min candle forming (potential entry before 15m close)
  • 2 green + 1 red 5-min candles = 15-min candle likely has upper wick (weakness)
  • Alternating green/red 5-min candles = 15-min doji forming (indecision, no trade)
Research Insight: Timeframe Confluence

Studies by Bulkowski (2008) and Nison (2001) consistently show that candlestick patterns confirmed by higher-timeframe structure have success rates 15-25 percentage points higher than single-timeframe patterns. The additional filter of volume confirmation can add another 10-15 points. This is why professional traders insist on confluence before entering any trade.

Multi-Timeframe Candle Confluence

A single candlestick pattern in isolation has moderate reliability. The same pattern at a level that matters on a higher timeframe becomes a high-probability signal. Multi-timeframe confluence means aligning candlestick signals across daily, hourly, and intraday charts to filter out noise and trade only the strongest setups.

  • Daily chart sets the context: A daily hammer at a major support level tells you the higher timeframe favors a bounce. This is your directional bias for the day.
  • Hourly chart identifies the setup: Within that daily hammer, the hourly chart may show a double bottom or bullish engulfing pattern forming. This narrows your entry window.
  • 5-minute chart triggers the entry: A bullish engulfing on the 5-minute chart, occurring at the hourly double bottom, which itself is the wick of a daily hammer at support — this is triple-timeframe confluence. The probability of this setup working is significantly higher than any single-timeframe signal.
  • Volume must agree: The higher-timeframe candle should show above-average volume (commitment). The lower-timeframe entry candle should also show increasing volume (confirmation). Low volume at any level weakens the signal.
  • The hierarchy rule: Never trade a lower-timeframe signal that contradicts the higher timeframe. A 5-minute bullish engulfing means nothing if the daily chart shows a bearish breakdown. Always defer to the higher timeframe for bias and use the lower timeframe only for entry timing.
