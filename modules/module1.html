MODULE 01

How Markets Really Work

Before you place a single trade, you need to understand the machinery behind every transaction. This module pulls back the curtain on market microstructure — the exchanges, intermediaries, and order mechanics that determine whether you get filled at the price you want or get quietly fleeced by the spread.

1.1 The Ecosystem

Financial markets are not a single monolithic entity. They are a network of competing venues, intermediaries, and electronic systems, all racing to match buyers with sellers in fractions of a second. Understanding who the players are — and how they profit — gives you an edge most retail traders never develop.

Exchanges

Exchanges are the regulated marketplaces where securities are listed and traded. The two dominant U.S. equity exchanges are:

  • NYSE (New York Stock Exchange) — The world's largest exchange by market capitalization. Uses a hybrid model with designated market makers (DMMs) on a physical trading floor alongside electronic matching.
  • NASDAQ — A fully electronic exchange and the birthplace of tech-stock trading. Operates a dealer-based system with multiple competing market makers per security.
  • CBOE, IEX, ARCA, BATS — Additional exchanges that compete for order flow by offering speed advantages or investor-protection mechanisms (IEX's famous "speed bump").

Market Makers

Market makers are firms that commit to providing liquidity by continuously posting both a bid (buy) and an ask (sell) price. They profit from the spread between these two prices. Without market makers, you might wait minutes or hours for someone to take the other side of your trade.

  • Obligated to maintain two-sided quotes during market hours
  • Profit from the bid-ask spread, not from directional bets
  • Major firms: Citadel Securities, Virtu Financial, GTS
  • Manage risk by hedging positions in real-time

ECNs (Electronic Communication Networks)

ECNs are automated systems that match buy and sell orders outside traditional exchanges. They allow trading during extended hours and often provide price improvement for large institutional orders. Examples include Instinet and SelectNet.

Pro Tip

When you "buy Apple stock," your order may be routed through 3-4 different venues before being filled. Your broker's routing algorithm (or payment-for-order-flow arrangement) determines where it goes. This matters more than most retail traders realize.

1.2 Order Types

Choosing the right order type is one of the most overlooked skills in trading. Each type gives you a different blend of speed, price control, and certainty. Here is every order type you need to master.

Order Type Execution Price Guarantee Best For
Market Order Immediate — fills at best available price None. You accept whatever the market gives you. Getting in/out fast when speed > price
Limit Order Only at your specified price or better Yes — guaranteed price or better (if filled) Controlling entry/exit price precisely
Stop Order Becomes a market order when stop price is reached None once triggered — fills at market Protecting profits or cutting losses
Stop-Limit Order Becomes a limit order when stop price is reached Yes — but may not fill if price gaps through Protecting against gaps with price control
Trailing Stop Stop price trails the market by a set amount None once triggered Locking in gains while letting winners run

Warning: Market Orders in Low Liquidity

Never use market orders on low-volume stocks or during pre/post-market hours. With thin order books, a market order can fill 2-5% away from the last traded price. This is called slippage, and it can destroy an otherwise good trade.

1.3 The Bid-Ask Spread

The spread is the silent tax on every trade you make. Understanding it is the difference between a profitable strategy on paper and a losing one in practice.

Definitions

  • Bid Price — The highest price a buyer is currently willing to pay. If you sell with a market order, you get the bid.
  • Ask Price — The lowest price a seller is currently willing to accept. If you buy with a market order, you pay the ask.
  • Spread — The difference between ask and bid. This is the market maker's profit and your cost of doing business.
  • Mid Price — (Bid + Ask) / 2. The theoretical "fair value" of the security at that moment.

Why the Spread Matters for Day Traders

If you trade a stock with a $0.05 spread and target $0.20 profit, you are already giving up 25% of your potential gain just to enter and exit. Scalpers should only trade stocks with penny-wide spreads and massive volume. Always check the Level 2 quotes before entering a trade.

1.4 How a Trade Actually Executes

Every time you click "Buy" or "Sell," a cascade of events happens in milliseconds. Here is exactly what occurs from click to fill.

Step-by-Step: Anatomy of a Trade

  1. Step 1: Order Submission — You place a buy order for 100 shares of AAPL at market through your broker's platform. The order is encrypted and sent to your broker's order management system (OMS).
  2. Step 2: Order Routing — Your broker's Smart Order Router (SOR) evaluates available venues — NYSE, NASDAQ, ARCA, BATS, dark pools — and selects the venue offering the best available price and fastest fill probability.
  3. Step 3: Venue Matching — The order arrives at the exchange's matching engine. If there is a resting sell order at or below your limit (or at the best ask for a market order), the engine pairs the two orders.
  4. Step 4: Execution — The trade is executed. Both the buyer's and seller's brokers receive an execution report confirming the price, quantity, and timestamp.
  5. Step 5: Clearing — The National Securities Clearing Corporation (NSCC) steps in as the central counterparty, guaranteeing the trade. It nets all trades for the day to minimize the actual shares and cash that need to move.
  6. Step 6: Settlement (T+1) — One business day after the trade, actual ownership of the shares is transferred and cash is debited/credited via the Depository Trust Company (DTC).
Settlement Risk: Why It Matters

If you sell a stock and immediately try to use those funds to buy another stock, you are trading with unsettled cash. Do this repeatedly in a cash account and you will trigger a good faith violation. Three violations in 12 months and your broker restricts your account. Margin accounts avoid this, but come with their own risks.

Reading the Book

  • Stacked size at $150.06 (3,500 shares) — This is a potential support level. Large resting bid orders suggest buyers are willing to absorb selling pressure here.
  • Stacked size at $150.17 (4,000 shares) — This is a potential resistance level. A large resting ask means sellers are stacked up, ready to dump shares at this price.
  • Thin levels — The $150.10 bid (200 shares) and $150.11 ask (350 shares) are the inner market. Because they are thin, a single large order could blow through them, causing the price to jump.
