MODULE 01

How Markets Really Work

Before you place a single trade, you need to understand the machinery behind every transaction. This module pulls back the curtain on market microstructure — the exchanges, intermediaries, and order mechanics that determine whether you get filled at the price you want or get quietly fleeced by the spread.

1.1 The Ecosystem

Financial markets are not a single monolithic entity. They are a network of competing venues, intermediaries, and electronic systems, all racing to match buyers with sellers in fractions of a second. Understanding who the players are — and how they profit — gives you an edge most retail traders never develop.

Exchanges

Exchanges are the regulated marketplaces where securities are listed and traded. The two dominant U.S. equity exchanges are:

  • NYSE (New York Stock Exchange) — The world's largest exchange by market capitalization. Uses a hybrid model with designated market makers (DMMs) on a physical trading floor alongside electronic matching.
  • NASDAQ — A fully electronic exchange and the birthplace of tech-stock trading. Operates a dealer-based system with multiple competing market makers per security.
  • CBOE, IEX, ARCA, BATS — Additional exchanges that compete for order flow by offering speed advantages or investor-protection mechanisms (IEX's famous "speed bump").

Market Makers

Market makers are firms that commit to providing liquidity by continuously posting both a bid (buy) and an ask (sell) price. They profit from the spread between these two prices. Without market makers, you might wait minutes or hours for someone to take the other side of your trade.

  • Obligated to maintain two-sided quotes during market hours
  • Profit from the bid-ask spread, not from directional bets
  • Major firms: Citadel Securities, Virtu Financial, GTS
  • Manage risk by hedging positions in real-time

ECNs (Electronic Communication Networks)

ECNs are automated systems that match buy and sell orders outside traditional exchanges. They allow trading during extended hours and often provide price improvement for large institutional orders. Examples include Instinet and SelectNet.

Pro Tip

When you "buy Apple stock," your order may be routed through 3-4 different venues before being filled. Your broker's routing algorithm (or payment-for-order-flow arrangement) determines where it goes. This matters more than most retail traders realize.

How Your Order Travels

Your order may be routed to any of these venues depending on your broker's smart order router.

1.2 Order Types

Choosing the right order type is one of the most overlooked skills in trading. Each type gives you a different blend of speed, price control, and certainty. Here is every order type you need to master.

Order Type Execution Price Guarantee Best For
Market Order Immediate — fills at best available price None. You accept whatever the market gives you. Getting in/out fast when speed > price
Limit Order Only at your specified price or better Yes — guaranteed price or better (if filled) Controlling entry/exit price precisely

Warning: Market Orders in Low Liquidity

Never use market orders on low-volume stocks or during pre/post-market hours. With thin order books, a market order can fill 2-5% away from the last traded price. This is called slippage, and it can destroy an otherwise good trade.

1.3 The Bid-Ask Spread

The spread is the silent tax on every trade you make. Understanding it is the difference between a profitable strategy on paper and a losing one in practice.

Definitions

  • Bid Price — The highest price a buyer is currently willing to pay. If you sell with a market order, you get the bid.
  • Ask Price — The lowest price a seller is currently willing to accept. If you buy with a market order, you pay the ask.
  • Spread — The difference between ask and bid. This is the market maker's profit and your cost of doing business.
  • Mid Price — (Bid + Ask) / 2. The theoretical "fair value" of the security at that moment.

Why the Spread Matters for Day Traders

If you trade a stock with a $0.05 spread and target $0.20 profit, you are already giving up 25% of your potential gain just to enter and exit. Scalpers should only trade stocks with penny-wide spreads and massive volume. Always check the Level 2 quotes before entering a trade.

1.4 How a Trade Actually Executes

Every time you click "Buy" or "Sell," a cascade of events happens in milliseconds. Here is exactly what occurs from click to fill.

Step-by-Step: Anatomy of a Trade

  1. Step 1: Order Submission — You place a buy order for 100 shares of AAPL at market through your broker's platform. The order is encrypted and sent to your broker's order management system (OMS).
  2. Step 2: Order Routing — Your broker's Smart Order Router (SOR) evaluates available venues — NYSE, NASDAQ, ARCA, BATS, dark pools — and selects the venue offering the best available price and fastest fill probability.
  3. Step 3: Venue Matching — The order arrives at the exchange's matching engine. If there is a resting sell order at or below your limit (or at the best ask for a market order), the engine pairs the two orders.
  4. Step 4: Execution — The trade is executed. Both the buyer's and seller's brokers receive an execution report confirming the price, quantity, and timestamp.
  5. Step 5: Clearing — The National Securities Clearing Corporation (NSCC) steps in as the central counterparty, guaranteeing the trade. It nets all trades for the day to minimize the actual shares and cash that need to move.
  6. Step 6: Settlement (T+1) — One business day after the trade, actual ownership of the shares is transferred and cash is debited/credited via the Depository Trust Company (DTC).
Settlement Risk: Why It Matters

If you sell a stock and immediately try to use those funds to buy another stock, you are trading with unsettled cash. Do this repeatedly in a cash account and you will trigger a good faith violation. Three violations in 12 months and your broker restricts your account. Margin accounts avoid this, but come with their own risks.

1.6 Dark Pools & Payment for Order Flow

Approximately 40-50% of all U.S. equity volume now trades off-exchange — in dark pools and through wholesalers. Understanding this hidden layer of market structure is essential for any serious trader.

What Are Dark Pools?

Dark pools are private, off-exchange trading venues where buy and sell orders are matched without displaying quotes publicly. They were originally created for institutional investors to execute large block trades without moving the market. Today, there are over 50 active dark pools in the U.S.

  • ATS (Alternative Trading Systems) — The regulatory classification for dark pools under SEC Regulation ATS
  • No pre-trade transparency — Orders are hidden until after execution, preventing front-running
  • Price improvement possible — Trades can execute at the midpoint of the NBBO, saving you half the spread
  • Major dark pools: Citadel Connect, Virtu Financial, UBS ATS, Goldman Sachs Sigma X, Morgan Stanley MS Pool

Payment for Order Flow (PFOF)

When you place an order on a commission-free broker (Robinhood, Schwab, E*TRADE), your broker does not send it to the NYSE or NASDAQ. Instead, it sells your order to a wholesale market maker like Citadel Securities or Virtu Financial. The wholesaler pays the broker a small fee per share and then fills your order internally.

  • How it works: Broker receives $0.002-$0.004 per share from the wholesaler for routing your order to them
  • Price improvement claim: Wholesalers claim they fill orders at slightly better prices than the NBBO (National Best Bid and Offer)
  • The concern: Your orders are not competing in the public market. The wholesaler sees your order before anyone else and can trade around it
  • SEC Rule 606: Brokers must disclose where they route orders and how much they receive in payment — check your broker's 606 report

Where Retail Orders Actually Go

The PFOF Debate

Proponents say PFOF funds commission-free trading and provides price improvement. Critics (including some SEC officials) argue it creates conflicts of interest and may result in worse execution for retail traders. As a day trader, consider using a broker with direct-access routing (Interactive Brokers, Lightspeed) where you control where your orders go.

1.7 Regulation NMS & Key Market Rules

Regulation NMS (National Market System), adopted by the SEC in 2005, is the backbone of modern U.S. equity market structure. Every trader benefits from understanding the rules that govern how their orders are handled.

Key Reg NMS Rules

  • Order Protection Rule (Rule 611) — Prohibits "trade-throughs" — executing a trade at a price worse than a protected quote displayed on another exchange. This ensures you get the National Best Bid and Offer (NBBO).
  • Access Rule (Rule 610) — Limits the fees exchanges can charge for accessing their quotes to $0.003 per share (the "access fee cap"). This prevents exchanges from posting the best price but making it prohibitively expensive to access.
  • Sub-Penny Rule (Rule 612) — Prohibits displaying quotes in increments finer than $0.01 for stocks priced above $1.00. This prevents market makers from stepping ahead of your limit order by $0.001.
  • Market Data Rules (Rules 601, 602, 603) — Require the consolidation and distribution of market data through Securities Information Processors (SIPs).

The Pattern Day Trader (PDT) Rule

FINRA Rule 4210 requires that any trader who executes 4 or more day trades within 5 business days in a margin account must maintain a minimum equity of $25,000. If your account falls below this threshold, you cannot day trade until it is replenished.

Account Type Day Trade Limit Minimum Equity Buying Power
Cash Account Unlimited (with settled cash) None 1x settled cash
Margin < $25K 3 day trades per 5 rolling days $2,000 2x maintenance margin

Workarounds for the PDT Rule

If you have under $25K: (1) Use a cash account with settled funds. (2) Trade futures, which are exempt from PDT. (3) Use multiple broker accounts to spread day trades. (4) Trade options (careful with settlement rules). (5) Trade in a prop firm account, which often has different rules.

SEC Proposed Reg NMS Reforms (2023–2024)

The SEC has proposed significant updates to Regulation NMS that could reshape equity market structure. Key proposals include: (1) Sub-penny pricing — allowing tick sizes as small as $0.001 for actively traded stocks, which could tighten spreads further. (2) Order Competition Rule — requiring marketable retail orders to be exposed to open competition via auctions before being filled by wholesalers, potentially ending the current PFOF model. (3) Best Execution Standard — establishing a federal best-execution rule for broker-dealers, replacing the patchwork of FINRA guidance. These reforms remain under deliberation and may be modified, but they signal a shift toward greater transparency and competition. Stay informed — changes to market structure directly affect your execution quality and costs.

1.8 High-Frequency Trading & Your Edge

High-frequency trading (HFT) firms account for roughly 50% of U.S. equity volume. They operate at microsecond speeds with co-located servers directly at the exchange. Here is what you need to know — and why you still have an edge.

What HFT Firms Do

  • Market making — Providing liquidity by continuously quoting bids and asks, profiting from the spread thousands of times per second
  • Statistical arbitrage — Exploiting tiny price discrepancies between related securities or venues (e.g., SPY vs ES futures)
  • Latency arbitrage — Racing to exploit price differences across exchanges before the SIP consolidates the data (the "speed tax" on slower traders)
  • Momentum ignition — Placing rapid orders to trigger other algorithms into buying or selling, then trading against them

Where Retail Still Wins

HFT excels at microsecond execution but has significant limitations. As a retail day trader, you have edges that algorithms struggle to replicate:

  • No obligation to trade — HFT firms must constantly deploy capital. You can sit in cash all day waiting for the perfect setup.
  • Small size advantage — A 100-share order has zero market impact. An HFT firm moving millions of shares must work around their own footprint.
  • Holding period flexibility — You can hold for minutes to hours. HFT holding periods are seconds or less. You occupy a different niche.
  • Pattern recognition on higher timeframes — 5-minute and daily chart patterns driven by human psychology are not easily arbitraged away by speed.
  • News interpretation — Natural language processing has improved, but human judgment on complex earnings calls or regulatory news still holds value.
Coexist, Don't Compete

You will never beat an HFT firm on speed. That is fine — you do not need to. Scalping for pennies is their game. Your game is reading price action, managing risk, and exploiting setups that unfold over minutes, not microseconds. The key is choosing strategies where speed is not the primary edge.
