7.1 Strategy Overview

No single strategy works in all market conditions. The best traders maintain a toolkit of approaches and deploy the right one for the current environment. This module covers six core strategies every day trader should understand.

7.2 Momentum / Trend Following

Momentum trading is the art of riding strong directional moves. You identify stocks or instruments that are moving with above-average volume and enter on pullbacks within the trend, rather than chasing the initial move.

How to Identify Momentum

  • Volume surge — Current volume is 2–5x the average for that time of day
  • Price breaking out of a range — New intraday highs or lows with conviction
  • Stacked moving averages — 9 EMA above 20 EMA above VWAP in an uptrend
  • Higher highs and higher lows on the 5-minute chart
  • Catalyst — News, earnings, or sector rotation driving the move
Momentum trend with pullback entries near the moving average

Key Rules for Momentum Trading

  • Always trade with the trend — Never try to pick tops or bottoms in a strong move
  • Wait for pullbacks — Enter when price retraces to the 9 or 20 EMA, not on the extension
  • Use trailing stops — Move your stop to the low of the prior pullback as the trend progresses
  • Confirm with volume — Pullbacks should be on lighter volume; the resume should see volume increase
  • Know when the trend is over — A break below the most recent higher low signals a shift
Pro Tip: The First Pullback Is the Best

The first pullback in a new trend has the highest probability of continuing. By the third or fourth pullback, the trend is often exhausted and the risk of reversal increases dramatically.

7.3 Breakout Trading

Breakout trading involves entering a position when price moves decisively beyond a defined level of support or resistance, usually after a period of consolidation. The key is distinguishing real breakouts from false ones.

Identifying Consolidation Patterns

  • Triangles — Ascending, descending, or symmetrical patterns with converging trendlines
  • Flags & Pennants — Brief pauses after a strong move, with a slight counter-trend drift
  • Flat-top breakouts — A horizontal resistance level tested 2–3 times with higher lows forming below it
  • Tight range days — When the daily range compresses to less than half its average
Ascending triangle breakout with volume confirmation

Avoiding False Breakouts

Real Breakout Signs

  • Volume 2x+ the average at the breakout bar
  • Strong close near the high of the breakout candle
  • Price holds above the breakout level on a retest
  • Occurs in the direction of the larger trend

False Breakout Signs

  • Low or average volume on the break
  • Long upper wick — sellers immediately reject the move
  • Price quickly falls back into the range
  • Occurs against the larger trend or at end of day
The #1 Breakout Mistake

Entering the moment price touches resistance. Wait for the candle to close above the level. Many "breakouts" are just upper wicks that get immediately sold. Patience pays.

7.4 Opening Range Breakout (ORB)

The Opening Range Breakout is one of the most popular and beginner-friendly day trading strategies. You define the high and low of the first 15 or 30 minutes of trading, then enter when price breaks above or below that range.

ORB Execution Steps

  • Step 1: Wait — Let the first 15–30 minutes play out. Do not trade during this period.
  • Step 2: Mark the range — Identify the high and low of the opening range on your chart.
  • Step 3: Set alerts — Place alerts at the ORB high and ORB low levels.
  • Step 4: Enter on break — Go long on a break above the ORB high (or short below the ORB low) with volume confirmation.
  • Step 5: Manage the trade — Stop loss inside the range (typically the opposite end or the midpoint). Target 1:1 to 2:1 R:R.
Opening Range Breakout — long entry on break above the 30-minute opening range high
ORB Works Best on Gap Days

The ORB strategy has the highest win rate when a stock gaps up or down significantly at the open. The gap creates directional momentum, and the opening range breakout aligns with that momentum.

7.5 VWAP Strategies

VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price) is the single most important indicator for intraday traders. It represents the average price weighted by volume and acts as a magnet for price. Institutional traders benchmark their executions against VWAP, making it a self-fulfilling support/resistance level.

Three Core VWAP Setups

VWAP Bounce (Long)

  • Price is above VWAP (bullish bias)
  • Price pulls back to test VWAP
  • Look for a bullish candle at VWAP
  • Enter long with stop below VWAP

VWAP Rejection (Short)

  • Price is below VWAP (bearish bias)
  • Price rallies up to test VWAP
  • Look for a bearish candle at VWAP
  • Enter short with stop above VWAP

VWAP Reclaim

A powerful setup occurs when price breaks below VWAP, trades underneath it for a period, then reclaims it with volume. This signals that sellers have been exhausted and buyers are taking control. Enter long on the reclaim candle with a stop below the recent low under VWAP.

VWAP Bounce and VWAP Reclaim setups on an intraday chart
VWAP Resets Daily

Unlike moving averages, VWAP resets at the start of each trading session. This makes it uniquely suited for intraday decisions. Never use yesterday's VWAP for today's trades.

7.6 Scalping

Scalping is the fastest form of day trading, aiming to capture small price movements over very short timeframes — often just a few cents per share. Scalpers may take dozens of trades per day, relying on a high win rate and rapid execution.

Scalping Essentials

  • Speed is everything — Hotkeys and direct-access routing are non-negotiable
  • Level 2 mastery — You must read the order book in real time to see where liquidity sits
  • Small position sizes — Risk must be tiny per trade because your edge comes from many small wins
  • High win rate required — You need 60%+ win rate because your risk/reward is close to 1:1
  • Tight spreads only — Trade liquid stocks with penny-wide spreads; wide spreads destroy scalping profits
  • Commission awareness — With dozens of trades, commissions add up fast
Commissions Will Eat Your Profits

If you scalp 50 trades per day at $5 per round-trip commission, that is $250/day in costs before you make a single dollar. Scalping only makes sense with commission-free or per-share pricing from a direct-access broker. Do the math before committing to this strategy.

Not for Beginners

Scalping requires the fastest reflexes, deepest market understanding, and most expensive tools. It is the last strategy you should attempt, not the first. Build your foundation with ORB or VWAP strategies before considering scalping.

7.7 Mean Reversion

Mean reversion is based on the idea that price tends to return to its average after extreme moves. When a stock becomes overextended — too far from VWAP, with RSI at extremes — there is a statistical tendency for it to snap back.

Identifying Mean Reversion Setups

  • RSI at extremes — Above 80 or below 20 on the 5-minute chart
  • Far from VWAP — Price is 2+ standard deviations away from VWAP
  • Parabolic move — Nearly vertical price action on heavy volume, often from a news catalyst
  • Exhaustion candles — Long wicks showing rejection at the extreme
  • Volume climax — A massive volume spike at the peak, often the "blow-off top"

Mean Reversion Trade Plan

  • Entry: Fade the extended move once you see exhaustion (rejection candle, volume climax)
  • Stop: Just beyond the extreme — above the high for shorts, below the low for longs
  • Target: VWAP or the 20 EMA — the "mean" you expect price to revert to
  • Size: Smaller than your momentum trades, because you are trading against the current move
Beware of Catching Falling Knives

Not every overextended move reverses. Some stocks are overextended because the move is real — a buyout, FDA approval, or short squeeze. Always wait for confirmation of exhaustion before entering. "Cheap" can always get cheaper.

Combine With Time of Day

Mean reversion works best during the midday session (11:30 AM – 2:00 PM ET) when momentum traders have finished and stocks tend to range and revert. Avoid fading moves during the opening hour, when momentum is strongest.
