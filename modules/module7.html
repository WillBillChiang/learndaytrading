7.1 Strategy Overview

No single strategy works in all market conditions. The best traders maintain a toolkit of approaches and deploy the right one for the current environment. This module covers six core strategies every day trader should understand.

Start With One

Master a single strategy before adding others. It is far better to be an expert in one setup than mediocre in five. Once you are consistently profitable, branch out.

7.2 Momentum / Trend Following

Momentum trading is the art of riding strong directional moves. You identify stocks or instruments that are moving with above-average volume and enter on pullbacks within the trend, rather than chasing the initial move.

How to Identify Momentum

  • Volume surge — Current volume is 2–5x the average for that time of day
  • Price breaking out of a range — New intraday highs or lows with conviction
  • Stacked moving averages — 9 EMA above 20 EMA above VWAP in an uptrend
  • Higher highs and higher lows on the 5-minute chart
  • Catalyst — News, earnings, or sector rotation driving the move
Momentum trend with pullback entries near the moving average

Key Rules for Momentum Trading

  • Always trade with the trend — Never try to pick tops or bottoms in a strong move
  • Wait for pullbacks — Enter when price retraces to the 9 or 20 EMA, not on the extension
  • Use trailing stops — Move your stop to the low of the prior pullback as the trend progresses
  • Confirm with volume — Pullbacks should be on lighter volume; the resume should see volume increase
  • Know when the trend is over — A break below the most recent higher low signals a shift
Pro Tip: The First Pullback Is the Best

The first pullback in a new trend has the highest probability of continuing. By the third or fourth pullback, the trend is often exhausted and the risk of reversal increases dramatically.

7.3 Breakout Trading

Breakout trading involves entering a position when price moves decisively beyond a defined level of support or resistance, usually after a period of consolidation. The key is distinguishing real breakouts from false ones.

Identifying Consolidation Patterns

  • Triangles — Ascending, descending, or symmetrical patterns with converging trendlines
  • Flags & Pennants — Brief pauses after a strong move, with a slight counter-trend drift
  • Flat-top breakouts — A horizontal resistance level tested 2–3 times with higher lows forming below it
  • Tight range days — When the daily range compresses to less than half its average
Ascending triangle breakout with volume confirmation

Avoiding False Breakouts

Real Breakout Signs

  • Volume 2x+ the average at the breakout bar
  • Strong close near the high of the breakout candle
  • Price holds above the breakout level on a retest
  • Occurs in the direction of the larger trend

False Breakout Signs

  • Low or average volume on the break
  • Long upper wick — sellers immediately reject the move
  • Price quickly falls back into the range
  • Occurs against the larger trend or at end of day
The #1 Breakout Mistake

Entering the moment price touches resistance. Wait for the candle to close above the level. Many "breakouts" are just upper wicks that get immediately sold. Patience pays.

7.4 Opening Range Breakout (ORB)

The Opening Range Breakout is one of the most popular and beginner-friendly day trading strategies. You define the high and low of the first 15 or 30 minutes of trading, then enter when price breaks above or below that range.

ORB Execution Steps

  • Step 1: Wait — Let the first 15–30 minutes play out. Do not trade during this period.
  • Step 2: Mark the range — Identify the high and low of the opening range on your chart.
  • Step 3: Set alerts — Place alerts at the ORB high and ORB low levels.
  • Step 4: Enter on break — Go long on a break above the ORB high (or short below the ORB low) with volume confirmation.
  • Step 5: Manage the trade — Stop loss inside the range (typically the opposite end or the midpoint). Target 1:1 to 2:1 R:R.

ORB Works Best on Gap Days

The ORB strategy has the highest win rate when a stock gaps up or down significantly at the open. The gap creates directional momentum, and the opening range breakout aligns with that momentum.

7.5 VWAP Strategies

VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price) is the single most important indicator for intraday traders. It represents the average price weighted by volume and acts as a magnet for price. Institutional traders benchmark their executions against VWAP, making it a self-fulfilling support/resistance level.

Three Core VWAP Setups

VWAP Bounce (Long)

  • Price is above VWAP (bullish bias)
  • Price pulls back to test VWAP
  • Look for a bullish candle at VWAP
  • Enter long with stop below VWAP

VWAP Rejection (Short)

  • Price is below VWAP (bearish bias)
  • Price rallies up to test VWAP
  • Look for a bearish candle at VWAP
  • Enter short with stop above VWAP

VWAP Reclaim

A powerful setup occurs when price breaks below VWAP, trades underneath it for a period, then reclaims it with volume. This signals that sellers have been exhausted and buyers are taking control. Enter long on the reclaim candle with a stop below the recent low under VWAP.

VWAP Bounce and VWAP Reclaim setups on an intraday chart
VWAP Resets Daily

Unlike moving averages, VWAP resets at the start of each trading session. This makes it uniquely suited for intraday decisions. Never use yesterday's VWAP for today's trades.

7.6 Scalping

Scalping is the fastest form of day trading, aiming to capture small price movements over very short timeframes — often just a few cents per share. Scalpers may take dozens of trades per day, relying on a high win rate and rapid execution.

Scalping Essentials

  • Speed is everything — Hotkeys and direct-access routing are non-negotiable
  • Level 2 mastery — You must read the order book in real time to see where liquidity sits
  • Small position sizes — Risk must be tiny per trade because your edge comes from many small wins
  • High win rate required — You need 60%+ win rate because your risk/reward is close to 1:1
  • Tight spreads only — Trade liquid stocks with penny-wide spreads; wide spreads destroy scalping profits
  • Commission awareness — With dozens of trades, commissions add up fast
Commissions Will Eat Your Profits

If you scalp 50 trades per day at $5 per round-trip commission, that is $250/day in costs before you make a single dollar. Scalping only makes sense with commission-free or per-share pricing from a direct-access broker. Do the math before committing to this strategy.

Not for Beginners

Scalping requires the fastest reflexes, deepest market understanding, and most expensive tools. It is the last strategy you should attempt, not the first. Build your foundation with ORB or VWAP strategies before considering scalping.

7.7 Mean Reversion

Mean reversion is based on the idea that price tends to return to its average after extreme moves. When a stock becomes overextended — too far from VWAP, with RSI at extremes — there is a statistical tendency for it to snap back.

Identifying Mean Reversion Setups

  • RSI at extremes — Above 80 or below 20 on the 5-minute chart
  • Far from VWAP — Price is 2+ standard deviations away from VWAP
  • Parabolic move — Nearly vertical price action on heavy volume, often from a news catalyst
  • Exhaustion candles — Long wicks showing rejection at the extreme
  • Volume climax — A massive volume spike at the peak, often the "blow-off top"

Mean Reversion Trade Plan

  • Entry: Fade the extended move once you see exhaustion (rejection candle, volume climax)
  • Stop: Just beyond the extreme — above the high for shorts, below the low for longs
  • Target: VWAP or the 20 EMA — the "mean" you expect price to revert to
  • Size: Smaller than your momentum trades, because you are trading against the current move
Beware of Catching Falling Knives

Not every overextended move reverses. Some stocks are overextended because the move is real — a buyout, FDA approval, or short squeeze. Always wait for confirmation of exhaustion before entering. "Cheap" can always get cheaper.

Combine With Time of Day

Mean reversion works best during the midday session (11:30 AM – 2:00 PM ET) when momentum traders have finished and stocks tend to range and revert. Avoid fading moves during the opening hour, when momentum is strongest.

7.9 Market Context: Sector Rotation, Futures, & Volatility Timing

No strategy operates in a vacuum. The broader market context — which sectors are leading, what futures did overnight, and where we are in the volatility cycle — dramatically affects which setups work on any given day. Professional traders assess these macro factors before selecting their strategy for the session.

Sector Rotation and Its Impact on Individual Stocks

Money flows between sectors in a predictable pattern tied to the economic cycle. Understanding where we are in the rotation helps you choose which stocks to trade and which direction to bias.

Economic Phase Leading Sectors Lagging Sectors Day Trading Implication
Early Recovery Financials, Consumer Discretionary, Industrials Utilities, Healthcare, Consumer Staples Favor long momentum in cyclicals; avoid defensive names
Mid Expansion Technology, Industrials, Materials Utilities, Energy (early) Growth/tech momentum strategies dominate; breakout setups work well
Late Expansion Energy, Materials, Healthcare Technology, Consumer Discretionary Rotation out of tech begins; mean reversion in former leaders, momentum in commodities
Contraction / Recession Utilities, Healthcare, Consumer Staples Financials, Consumer Discretionary, Industrials Short setups dominate; defensive names for longs only; reduce overall exposure

Intraday sector rotation: Even within a single session, money rotates. Watch the sector ETFs (XLF, XLK, XLE, XLV, etc.) on a relative strength basis. If XLK is leading early but rolling over while XLE strengthens, individual tech stocks will face headwinds while energy names catch momentum. Trade in the direction of the sector flow, not against it.

Overnight Futures and Their Impact on the Open

U.S. equity index futures (/ES for S&P 500, /NQ for Nasdaq 100, /RTY for Russell 2000) trade nearly 24 hours. Their overnight movement is the single best predictor of the opening gap and the first 30 minutes of price action.

  • /ES and /NQ set the tone: If /ES is up 0.5% overnight, most large-cap stocks will gap up. If /NQ is down 1% while /ES is flat, expect tech-heavy names to gap down while other sectors hold.
  • Overnight range matters: A narrow overnight range (<0.3% on /ES) suggests a balanced open with potential for a range day. A wide overnight range (>1%) suggests a directional day with gap-and-go potential.
  • Overnight levels become intraday S/R: The overnight high, overnight low, and the settlement price (4:15 PM ET close) act as key support/resistance for the regular session. Many algorithmic systems reference these levels.
  • Globex volume analysis: High overnight volume validates the move; low volume suggests the gap may fade. Check the volume profile of the overnight session before the open.
Overnight Scenario Opening Bias Preferred Strategy
/ES up >0.5%, high volume, clear catalyst Gap and Go (bullish) Momentum / ORB continuation
/ES up >0.5%, low volume, no catalyst Potential gap fade Mean reversion / VWAP fade
/ES flat, narrow range Balanced / rotational Range trading / VWAP bounce
/ES down >0.5%, high volume, clear catalyst Gap and Go (bearish) Momentum short / breakdown
/ES down >0.5%, low volume, no catalyst Potential gap fade Mean reversion long / VWAP reclaim

ATR-Based Entry Timing

Not all breakouts are created equal. A stock that has already moved 80% of its Average True Range (ATR) by 10:00 AM has limited fuel remaining for the day. Conversely, a stock that has moved only 20% of its ATR by mid-morning has significant room for expansion. The ATR percentile tells you how much of the day’s expected range has already been consumed.

  • Formula: ATR% Consumed = (Current High - Current Low) / ATR(14) × 100
  • Below 40% consumed: The stock has room to run. Breakout and momentum entries have the best probability of continuation.
  • 40%–70% consumed: Moderate fuel remaining. Be selective — only take A+ setups with strong catalyst and volume.
  • Above 70% consumed: Most of the day’s range is used up. Avoid new breakout entries. Favor mean reversion or wait for the next session.
  • Exception: On abnormally volatile days (earnings, FOMC, CPI), the stock can exceed 2–3x its normal ATR. In these cases, the ATR percentile underestimates remaining potential.
Pre-Market Context Checklist

Before the opening bell, review: (1) /ES and /NQ overnight direction and volume, (2) sector ETF pre-market movers, (3) economic calendar for today’s events, (4) your watchlist stocks’ pre-market gaps and catalysts, (5) VIX level and direction. This 5-minute scan sets the context for every decision you make during the session.

7.10 VWAP Trading Strategies

VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price) is the institutional benchmark — the price at which the majority of volume has transacted during the session. Large funds measure execution quality against VWAP, which means it acts as a gravitational center for price. Mastering VWAP-based strategies gives you a window into how institutions are positioning.

Why VWAP Matters to Institutions

When a mutual fund needs to buy 500,000 shares, their algorithm benchmarks against VWAP. If they get filled below VWAP, that is a "good" execution. This institutional behavior creates predictable support and resistance at the VWAP level every single day, which retail traders can exploit.

Core VWAP Trading Setups

VWAP Bounce (Long)

  • Context: Stock is in an intraday uptrend, trading above VWAP
  • Setup: Price pulls back to VWAP on declining volume
  • Entry: Buy when price touches VWAP and prints a bullish reversal candle (hammer, engulfing)
  • Stop: 10-15 cents below VWAP (or below the pullback low)
  • Target: Prior swing high, or 2:1 R:R minimum

VWAP Fade (Short)

  • Context: Stock is in an intraday downtrend, trading below VWAP
  • Setup: Price rallies back up to VWAP on declining volume
  • Entry: Short when price reaches VWAP and prints a bearish rejection candle (shooting star, bearish engulfing)
  • Stop: 10-15 cents above VWAP (or above the rally high)
  • Target: Prior swing low, or 2:1 R:R minimum

VWAP Breakout

  • Context: Stock has been trading below VWAP, consolidating just underneath
  • Setup: Price breaks above VWAP with a strong candle and above-average volume
  • Entry: Buy the close of the breakout candle or the first pullback after the break
  • Stop: Below VWAP (the level should now act as support)
  • Target: High of day or the next technical resistance level

VWAP Breakdown

  • Context: Stock has been trading above VWAP, consolidating just above it
  • Setup: Price breaks below VWAP with a strong candle and above-average volume
  • Entry: Short the close of the breakdown candle or the first bounce that fails at VWAP
  • Stop: Above VWAP (the level should now act as resistance)
  • Target: Low of day or the next technical support level

VWAP Bounce Entry/Exit Rules

  • Rule 1: Only trade VWAP bounces when the daily trend is in your favor — longs in uptrends, shorts in downtrends
  • Rule 2: Wait for a confirming candle at VWAP — do not blindly buy the touch
  • Rule 3: Volume on the pullback to VWAP should be declining; volume on the bounce should increase
  • Rule 4: The first and second VWAP touches of the day have the highest probability; the third touch and beyond have diminishing reliability
  • Rule 5: Stop loss goes below VWAP for longs (above for shorts) — if VWAP fails as support/resistance, the trade thesis is broken
  • Rule 6: Take partial profits at the first resistance level and trail the remainder
VWAP Bounce setup with entry, stop loss, and target levels marked. Note declining volume on pullback and rising volume on bounce.
VWAP Standard Deviation Bands

Many platforms offer VWAP bands (1st and 2nd standard deviation). These act like Bollinger Bands around VWAP. The 1st deviation band often serves as a take-profit zone for VWAP bounce trades, while touches of the 2nd deviation band can signal mean reversion opportunities.

7.11 Opening Range Breakout (Advanced)

The Opening Range Breakout is one of the most researched strategies in day trading, with roots going back to Toby Crabel's pioneering work in the 1990s. His book Day Trading With Short Term Price Patterns and Opening Range Breakout demonstrated that the opening range captures the initial battle between overnight sentiment and morning order flow, creating a statistically significant reference point for the rest of the session.

ORB Timeframe Variants

Not all opening ranges are created equal. The timeframe you choose determines the tradeoff between signal quality and the number of opportunities you get. Here is how the three main variants compare.

ORB Variant Range Period Win Rate Avg R:R Best Used When Key Advantage
5-Min ORB 9:30 – 9:35 AM ~45-50% 2:1 – 3:1 High-volatility days, strong gap ups/downs, news catalysts Fastest entry; captures explosive momentum early
15-Min ORB 9:30 – 9:45 AM ~50-55% 1.5:1 – 2.5:1 Most market conditions; the "default" ORB for many traders Balances speed with noise filtration
30-Min ORB 9:30 – 10:00 AM ~55-60% 1:1 – 2:1 Choppy or low-volatility days; when you want higher confidence Highest win rate; filters out most false breakouts

ORB With Volume Confirmation

A breakout without volume is a trap waiting to happen. Always confirm ORB breakouts with a volume surge. The breakout candle should have at least 1.5x the average volume of the opening range candles. Without that volume confirmation, treat the breakout as suspect and either skip the trade or wait for a retest.

Failed ORB — The Reverse Trade

One of the highest-probability setups in day trading is the failed ORB. This occurs when price breaks above (or below) the opening range, fails to follow through, and reverses back inside the range. The failed breakout traps breakout traders, and their stop-loss orders fuel the reversal.

  • Identification: Price breaks the ORB high/low, moves 2-5 cents beyond, then immediately reverses back inside the range within 1-2 candles
  • Entry: Enter in the opposite direction once price closes back inside the opening range
  • Stop: Just beyond the failed breakout high/low (this is the tightest stop you will ever get)
  • Target: The opposite side of the opening range, then trail if momentum continues
  • Edge: Trapped breakout traders' stops add fuel to your trade — you are trading with the market makers

ORB + Gap Combination

The most powerful ORB setups occur when they align with a gap. Crabel's research showed that gaps provide directional bias, and the ORB gives you the trigger. Here is how to combine them for maximum edge:

  • Gap Up + ORB Long: Stock gaps up 3%+ on news/earnings. The 15-min opening range forms entirely above the prior close. A breakout above the ORB high is a continuation signal with the highest probability of follow-through.
  • Gap Up + ORB Short (Failed Gap): Stock gaps up but the ORB breaks to the downside. This signals the gap is being rejected and often leads to a full gap fill.
  • Gap Down + ORB Short: Stock gaps down on bad news. The ORB forms below the prior close. A breakdown below the ORB low confirms selling pressure and targets lower.
  • Gap Down + ORB Long (Gap Reversal): Stock gaps down but the ORB breaks to the upside. Short sellers get squeezed, and the resulting rally can be explosive.
Crabel's Key Insight

Toby Crabel found that the narrower the opening range relative to recent ranges, the more likely a significant breakout move would follow. A narrow range compresses energy like a coiled spring. When you see an unusually tight opening range, prepare for a big move — you just do not know the direction yet.

Choose Your ORB by Context

Use the 5-min ORB on days with strong catalysts and wide gaps, the 15-min ORB as your default on normal-volatility days, and the 30-min ORB on choppy days when you need more confirmation. Many experienced traders use the 15-min ORB but draw all three ranges on their chart for reference.

7.12 Order Flow Scalping

Order flow scalping is the practice of reading the raw order book (Level 2) and the transaction tape (Time & Sales) to make ultra-short-term trading decisions. Instead of relying on chart patterns that summarize past price action, order flow traders watch the actual buying and selling pressure in real time. This section provides a brief introduction — detailed coverage of tape reading and Level 2 analysis is covered in Module 11.

Hardest Strategy — Not for Beginners

Order flow scalping is widely considered the most difficult day trading strategy. It requires extensive screen time (often 1,000+ hours), a direct-access broker, expensive Level 2 data feeds, and reflexes sharpened through months of practice. Most profitable scalpers spent 6-12 months losing money before becoming consistent. Do not attempt this strategy until you are already profitable with at least one other approach.

Reading the Order Book for Absorption

Absorption is the key concept in order flow scalping. It occurs when large resting orders on one side of the book "absorb" aggressive orders from the other side without the price moving. This reveals hidden institutional interest and can predict short-term direction.

Bid Absorption (Bullish Signal)

  • What you see: A large bid (e.g., 50,000 shares) sits on the bid and does not move
  • What happens: Aggressive sellers keep hitting that bid with market orders, but the bid holds firm
  • What it means: A large buyer is accumulating shares — they want to buy at this price
  • How to trade: Go long with a stop just below the large bid. If the bid finally gets taken out, exit immediately
  • Target: The next resistance level on the ask side, or when you see ask absorption forming

Ask Absorption (Bearish Signal)

  • What you see: A large ask (e.g., 50,000 shares) sits on the ask and does not move
  • What happens: Aggressive buyers keep hitting that ask with market orders, but the ask holds firm
  • What it means: A large seller is distributing shares — they want to sell at this price
  • How to trade: Go short with a stop just above the large ask. If the ask gets taken out (bought through), exit immediately
  • Target: The next support level on the bid side, or when you see bid absorption forming

Minimum Requirements for Order Flow Scalping

  • Direct-access broker — You need sub-millisecond routing to ECNs (e.g., Lightspeed, Centerpoint, Interactive Brokers Pro). Retail brokers like Robinhood or Webull are not suitable.
  • Level 2 market data — Full depth-of-book data from all exchanges (NYSE Arca, NASDAQ, EDGX, etc.). Budget $50-150/month for data feeds.
  • Time & Sales (The Tape) — Real-time transaction data showing every print — price, size, and the exchange it occurred on.
  • Hotkey execution — Pre-programmed keys for instant buy/sell/flatten. You cannot afford to click through order entry windows.
  • Low commissions — Per-share pricing (typically $0.002-0.005/share) is essential because you trade large size for small moves.
  • Fast internet connection — Wired connection with low latency to your broker's servers. Wi-Fi introduces unacceptable jitter for scalping.

Scalping Risk Management Rules

  • Max loss per trade: 5-10 cents per share. If the trade is not working within seconds, get out.
  • Max daily loss: Set a hard daily stop. Many scalpers use $200-500 depending on account size.
  • Position sizing: Large enough that small moves matter, small enough that one loss does not hurt. Typically 1,000-5,000 shares for most scalpers.
  • No averaging down: If a scalp goes against you, take the loss. Adding to losers is the fastest way to blow up a scalping account.
Spoofing and Layering

Be aware that not all large orders on Level 2 are real. "Spoofing" (placing large orders with the intent to cancel before execution) is illegal but still occurs. Look for orders that repeatedly appear and disappear at the same level — these may be spoof orders designed to deceive. Real absorption shows the large order actually getting filled, tick by tick, on the Time & Sales tape.

7.13 Gap Trading Strategies

A gap occurs when a stock opens at a significantly different price than its prior close, creating a visible "gap" on the chart. Gaps are caused by overnight news, earnings, analyst upgrades/downgrades, or macroeconomic events. Understanding gap types and their statistical tendencies gives you a significant edge in the first hours of trading.

The Four Types of Gaps

Gap Type Description Fill Rate How to Trade
Common Gap Occurs in the absence of significant news, often within a trading range. Low volume on the gap. No clear catalyst. ~70% Fade the gap — trade in the direction of the fill. These gaps are noise and tend to fill quickly, often within the first 1-2 hours.
Breakaway Gap Occurs at the start of a new trend, breaking out of a consolidation pattern or range. High volume on the gap. Strong catalyst (earnings surprise, new product, FDA approval). ~30% Trade with the gap direction — do not fade. These gaps signal the beginning of a major move. Buy the breakout or the first pullback, and hold for a continuation.
Runaway / Continuation Gap Occurs mid-trend, representing renewed momentum. Volume is high but may not be as extreme as a breakaway gap. Often occurs after a brief consolidation within the trend. ~40% Trade with the trend — enter on the gap or on the first intraday pullback. These gaps confirm the trend is accelerating. Target the measured move (distance of the trend before the gap, projected from the gap).
Exhaustion Gap Occurs near the end of a trend after a long run-up or sell-off. Very high volume (climactic). The stock has already moved significantly in the same direction over recent days/weeks. ~65% Fade the gap once exhaustion is confirmed. Watch for a reversal candle on the 5-min chart within the first 30 minutes. This gap often marks the final burst of energy before a trend reversal.
Fill Rates Are General Guidance, Not Fixed Rules

The fill rate percentages above are approximations based on historical observations across broad market samples. They are not fixed rules. Actual fill rates vary significantly by market regime (trending vs. ranging), sector, market capitalization, catalyst strength, and the specific stock's volatility characteristics. Small-cap stocks exhibit different gap behavior than large-caps. Gaps during earnings season behave differently than gaps during quiet periods. Always track your own gap statistics to build personalized data that reflects the stocks and setups you actually trade.

Gap and Go Strategy

The Gap and Go strategy trades in the direction of the gap, expecting momentum to continue. It works best with breakaway and runaway gaps on high relative volume. Here is the execution plan:

  • Pre-market scan: Find stocks gapping 4%+ on above-average pre-market volume with a clear catalyst
  • Wait for the ORB: Let the first 5-15 minutes form the opening range. A gap without follow-through is dangerous.
  • Entry trigger: Buy when price breaks above the opening range high (for gap ups) with volume confirmation
  • Stop loss: Below the opening range low, or below VWAP if the range is too wide
  • Targets: Pre-market high as the first target, then trail using the 9 EMA on the 5-minute chart
  • Time limit: If the stock does not make a new high within 30 minutes of your entry, consider exiting. Gap and Go momentum fades after the first hour.

Gap Fade Strategy

The Gap Fade trades against the gap, expecting it to fill. This strategy works best with common gaps and exhaustion gaps on relatively low volume. Here is the execution plan:

  • Pre-market scan: Find stocks gapping on no real catalyst, or gapping into major resistance/support levels
  • Wait for failure: Watch the first 15-30 minutes. If the stock cannot hold above the opening price and starts to sell off, the gap is likely to fill.
  • Entry trigger: Short when price breaks below the opening range low (for gap-up fades), or long when price breaks above the opening range high (for gap-down fades)
  • Stop loss: Above the pre-market high (for short fades) or below the pre-market low (for long fades)
  • Target: The prior day's close (the gap fill level). Take partial profits at 50% of the gap fill distance.
  • Avoid fading: Never fade gaps caused by earnings, M&A news, or FDA decisions. These are information-driven breakaway gaps and are unlikely to fill.

Gap Trading Decision Framework

  • Step 1: Identify the gap type — Is there a catalyst? How much has the stock already moved in this direction? What is the volume like?
  • Step 2: Determine probability — Common/exhaustion gaps favor the fade; breakaway/runaway gaps favor continuation
  • Step 3: Wait for the opening range — Never trade a gap in the first 2 minutes. Let the ORB form to give you a reference level.
  • Step 4: Confirm with volume — The breakout from the ORB (in either direction) must have volume. Without it, stand aside.
  • Step 5: Set hard stops — Gap trades can reverse violently. Respect your stop or risk giving back the entire gap move and more.
Keep a Gap Statistics Log

Track every gap you trade: the gap percentage, catalyst (or lack thereof), gap type, whether it filled, and how long the fill took. After 50-100 gap trades, you will have your own personalized gap fill statistics that may be even more useful than generic numbers, because they reflect the specific stocks and setups you trade.

Earnings Gap Warning

Earnings gaps are among the most powerful and least likely to fill. A stock that gaps 10% on a strong earnings report will often continue higher for days or weeks. Attempting to fade an earnings gap is one of the most common ways new traders take large losses. Always check the economic calendar before fading any gap.
